## Position 2 — D-Shape (frets 9–13)

Position 2 of C major runs from fret 9 to fret 13 with the root at fret 10 on the D string and fret 13 on the B string. The span extends to fret 13 because of the B string's natural offset — the B string sits one semitone lower than expected in the standard EADGBE tuning.

## The B String Offset

The major scale (and all scales) have to account for the B string being tuned a half-step lower than the pattern would expect. In this position, the 7th (B) and root (C) appear at frets 12 and 13 on the B string — one fret higher than they would land on the G or D string. This is not an error; it is a consistent feature of standard guitar tuning that affects every scale shape at the G–B string boundary.

## The 7th at the Start of D and G Strings

Position 2 opens with the 7th degree on the D string (fret 9) and G string (fret 9). This means any ascending run through Position 2 from the low strings begins with a leading tone — a note that pulls powerfully toward the root on the next fret. Position 2 phrases that start low and rise naturally create this built-in tension-and-release.

## Connecting Positions 1 and 2

The overlap: the 2nd degree (D in C major) sits at fret 10 on the low E string — the first note of Position 2 on that string — and also at fret 10 on the high e string, which is the last note of Position 1. Move fluidly between the two positions by treating fret 10 on string 1 as the handoff point.

## Fingering

The D string starts at fret 9 (index), then 10 (middle), then 12 (pinky). The G string is the same. The low E and A strings skip fret 9 and start at fret 10. The B string extends to fret 13 — reach with the pinky while keeping the hand anchored around fret 10.

## What's Next

Position 2 introduces the upper-neck zone. Position 3 (next) centers on the 12th fret and opens on the 3rd degree — the first note of one of the major scale's two natural half-step pairs, immediately giving the position a recognizable harmonic character.
