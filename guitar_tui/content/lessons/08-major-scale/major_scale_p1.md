## The Major Scale on the Neck

The C major scale uses the notes C D E F G A B — the seven natural notes with no sharps or flats. Its interval formula (W–W–H–W–W–W–H) produces the bright, settled sound of the "do-re-mi" scale. Like the natural minor, it has five positions across the neck following the CAGED shapes.

Position 1 of C major has its root on the low E string at fret 8 — this is the E-shape position.

## Major vs Minor at the Same Position

Position 1 of C major occupies the same fret range (7–10) as Position 2 of A minor pentatonic and Position 2 of A natural minor. This is the relative major/minor relationship in action: all those scales share the same notes but treat different pitches as home.

## String-by-String

| String | Frets | Degrees |
|--------|-------|---------|
| 6 (low E) | 7, 8, 10 | 7, 1, 2 |
| 5 (A) | 7, 8, 10 | 3, 4, 5 |
| 4 (D) | 7, 9, 10 | 6, 7, 1 |
| 3 (G) | 7, 9, 10 | 2, 3, 4 |
| 2 (B) | 8, 10 | 5, 6 |
| 1 (high e) | 8, 10 | 1, 2 |

Most strings carry three notes; strings 2 and 1 carry two each (the high-string side of the shape is lighter). This is characteristic of the E-shape major position.

## The 7th Degree

The major scale's 7th degree (B in C major, at fret 7 on the low E string) is one semitone below the root. In melody, the 7th creates tension that resolves strongly upward to the root — the most powerful resolution in major key music. Practise the pattern: play fret 7 then fret 8 on string 6 (7th → root) and listen to the pull and release.

## Fingering

Index at fret 7, middle at 8, ring at 9, pinky at 10. The D string and G string both have notes at 7, 9, 10 (skipping 8) — use index, ring, pinky for those strings, letting middle rest.
