## Diatonic Chords of C Major

Stacking thirds on each degree of C major using only the notes C D E F G A B produces seven chords — the diatonic chords of the key. Every major key follows the same pattern of chord qualities:

| Degree | Numeral | Chord | Type |
|--------|---------|-------|------|
| 1 — C | **I** | C | major |
| 2 — D | **ii** | Dm | minor |
| 3 — E | **iii** | Em | minor |
| 4 — F | **IV** | F | major |
| 5 — G | **V** | G | major |
| 6 — A | **vi** | Am | minor |
| 7 — B | **vii°** | Bdim | diminished |

Pattern: **major – minor – minor – major – major – minor – diminished**.

## The Most Important Relationships

**I – IV – V** (C – F – G): the three major chords of the key. The I–IV–V is the most common chord progression in Western music. The V creates tension that resolves to I.

**vi** (Am): the relative minor. Progressions that move between I and vi (C–Am) toggle between the major key's home and its relative minor home. The vi–IV–I–V and I–V–vi–IV patterns are built from just these four chords.

**vii°** (Bdim): the least used in open-position guitar music but important in classical and jazz harmony. It creates strong tension toward I because its two notes (B and F) are the 7th and 4th of the C major scale — the two most tension-loaded tones.

## The I–IV–V–vi Relationship

These four chords dominate popular music:

| Numeral | In C | In G | In D |
|---------|------|------|------|
| I | C | G | D |
| IV | F | C | A |
| V | G | D | E |
| vi | Am | Em | Bm |

Once you know the diatonic chord quality pattern, you can derive the chords of any major key in seconds.
