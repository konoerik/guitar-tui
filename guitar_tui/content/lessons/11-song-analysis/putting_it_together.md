## The Full Workflow

By now you have all the pieces:

1. **Find the tonal centre** — the chord and note that function as home
2. **Identify major or minor** — the quality of the tonic chord
3. **Label chord functions** — Roman numerals in context
4. **Check for modal colour** — does the progression imply a mode?
5. **Select the scale** — pentatonic base, then refine with full scale or mode
6. **Identify any non-diatonic chords** — borrowed, secondary dominant, or modulation

This lesson walks through three complete analyses: a major-key pop song, a minor-key rock song, and a modal progression. Each uses a different pathway through the workflow.

## Analysis 1: *Brown Eyed Girl* (Van Morrison) — Major Key

**Key identification:** The song opens and ends on G. The chorus lands on G as its resolution point. Key = G major.

**Major or minor:** The G chord is major. The chorus feels bright and settled on G. Major.

**Chord functions:** G – C – G – D (verse), then G – C – G – D – G.
- G = I, C = IV, D = V.
- This is I – IV – V – I: the most fundamental major-key progression.

**Modal colour:** None. This is straightforward tonal harmony with a clear V – I cadence.

**Scale choice:** G major pentatonic or G major scale. The major pentatonic captures the song's brightness perfectly; adding the major scale gives more melodic options.

**What makes it sound the way it does:** The I – IV – V progression is maximally stable and resolved. Every chord has clear tonic or dominant function. The brightness comes from the major key and the clean resolution pattern.

## Analysis 2: *Paranoid* (Black Sabbath) — Minor Key

**Key identification:** The main riff centres on E. The E power chord (E5) is the starting and returning point of the riff.

**Major or minor:** E5 (power chord) is ambiguous — it has no 3rd. But the riff movement — E – D – E, then the descent through D – C – D – E — implies a minor tonality. The guitar solo uses the E minor pentatonic throughout. Key = E minor.

**Chord functions:** The riff uses E5 – D5 – E5 (i – bVII – i). The verse chord sequence adds G (bIII). Full verse: E5 – D5 – G5 – D5 – E5.
- E = i, D = bVII, G = bIII.
- This is i – bVII – bIII – bVII — all chords from the natural minor scale, moving in a flat-direction pattern.

**Modal colour:** No bII (so not Phrygian). No major IV (so not Dorian). The progression is straightforward E Aeolian (natural minor).

**Scale choice:** E minor pentatonic (for the riff feel), or E natural minor for full melodic options. The original solo uses the E minor pentatonic exclusively and aggressively.

**What makes it sound the way it does:** The bVII chord (D) gives the riff that driving, slightly unstable character. The absence of a conventional V chord (B major) means there is no conventional resolution — the tension never fully releases, giving the song its relentless energy.

## Analysis 3: *Another Brick in the Wall, Part 2* (Pink Floyd) — Dorian

**Key identification:** The main groove centres on Dm. The verse vamp is Dm only.

**Major or minor:** Dm is minor. Key = D minor (but which mode?).

**Chord functions:** Verse is just Dm (i). Chorus adds G major — the IV chord in D natural minor would be Gm (minor), but here it is G *major*.

**Modal colour:** Major IV in a minor key = Dorian. The G major chord (IV, natural major) against a D minor tonic is the Dorian fingerprint. Key = D Dorian.

**Scale choice:** D Dorian. The guitar solo (David Gilmour) uses the D minor pentatonic and D natural minor, but the natural 6th (B natural) of Dorian appears in the melody. The minor pentatonic is the primary tool; knowing it is Dorian tells you the B natural is available for added brightness.

**What makes it sound the way it does:** Dorian's natural 6th gives the groove a hopeful, funky energy that straight minor would not have. The G major chord (major IV) lifts the progression slightly — it sounds brighter than a minor iv would.

## Summary: The Questions to Ask Every Time

| Question | Answer gives you |
|----------|-----------------|
| What chord feels like home? | The tonic |
| Is that chord major or minor? | The key quality |
| What are the other chords relative to the tonic? | Roman numeral functions |
| Is there a modal signature chord? | The mode, if any |
| Which scale fits? | Your improvisation/melody tool |
| Are any chords non-diatonic? | Borrowed chords, secondary dominants, modulations |

Song analysis is a skill that improves with practice. Start with simple three-chord songs, work up to four-chord pop progressions, then tackle minor-key rock and modal music. After fifty analyses, the framework becomes automatic — you hear a progression and the answers appear without conscious effort.

That is the goal: turning theory into perception.

## Where to Go From Here

Song analysis is a living skill — it deepens with every song you work through. The tools in this curriculum (chord construction, diatonic harmony, pentatonic and modal scales, Roman numeral analysis) are the foundation. Apply them to songs you already play, to recordings you love, and to any new music you encounter. The framework works in every key and every genre — use it.
