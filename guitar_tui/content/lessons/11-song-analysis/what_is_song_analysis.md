## Why Analyze a Song?

Every guitarist eventually asks: *how do they know what to play?* The answer is not instinct or talent — it is analysis. Song analysis is the skill of listening to a piece of music and identifying its underlying structure: the key, the tonal quality (major or minor), the chord function, and the scale that fits.

Once you can analyze a song, you can:
- Figure out the chords by ear
- Choose the right scale for improvisation or soloing
- Understand why certain notes sound good over certain chords
- Transpose any song to any key without losing its character

This track teaches a repeatable workflow for doing all of this.

## The Four Questions

Every song analysis starts with the same four questions, answered in order:

**1. What is the key?**
The key is the home base — the note and chord everything resolves to. Most songs are in a single key throughout, or modulate (change key) for a section and return.

**2. Is it major or minor?**
The same key letter (e.g. A) can produce a completely different sound depending on whether the tonic chord is major (A) or minor (Am). This is the most important fork in the analysis.

**3. What are the chord functions?**
Once you know the key and quality, label each chord with a Roman numeral: I, IV, V, ii, vi, etc. This reveals the *function* of each chord, which transfers to any other key.

**4. What scale fits?**
The key and quality tell you the base scale. Modal color in the progression may point to a specific mode. The right scale is the one whose notes match the chords and whose characteristic notes work as melody.

## A Simple Example

Song: *Knockin' on Heaven's Door* (Bob Dylan)

The chords are G – D – Am – Am (verse) and G – D – C – C (chorus).

1. **Key?** G. The song starts and ends on G, and G feels like home.
2. **Major or minor?** Major. The G chord is major, and the progression uses D (V) and Am (ii), both natural to G major.
3. **Functions?** Verse: I – V – ii – ii. Chorus: I – V – IV – IV.
4. **Scale?** G major. The minor pentatonic also works (blues approach), but G major maps perfectly to all the chord tones.

You can now solo over this song, transpose it to any key, and understand why its changes feel the way they do.

## What You Need Before You Start

Song analysis is applied music theory. The more theory you know, the faster and more complete your analysis will be. Before working through this track you should be comfortable with:

- Reading Roman numerals (I, IV, V, ii, vi — covered in *Roman Numerals and Chord Functions*)
- The diatonic chord families for major and minor keys
- The sound and feel of major vs minor
- At least the pentatonic scales and the major/natural minor scale

The remaining lessons in this track walk through each step of the workflow in depth, then synthesize them into a complete analysis method.
