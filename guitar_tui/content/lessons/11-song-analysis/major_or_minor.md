## Why This Matters

C major and A minor share every note and every chord. The difference is which chord functions as the tonal centre. If a song is in C major, C is home. If it is in A minor, Am is home. This distinction changes everything: the emotional colour, the scale you use to solo, and how you label the chord functions.

Getting this wrong produces analyses that are technically valid but musically useless — calling a song "in C major" when it clearly resolves to Am leads you to choose the wrong scale and label the chords with the wrong Roman numerals.

## The Tonic Chord Quality

The most direct test: **what quality is the tonic chord?**

- If the chord everything resolves to is *major*, the song is in a major key.
- If the chord everything resolves to is *minor*, the song is in a minor key.

A natural minor key has a minor i chord, a minor iv chord, and a major V (or bVII, bVI — see below). A major key has a major I, a major IV, and a major V.

Listen for the tonic chord at the end of the song or the end of each verse. Its quality answers the question directly.

## The Harmonic Context

Sometimes the tonic is not immediately clear. Look at the chords surrounding it:

**Major key context:**
- V – I cadence (e.g. G – C): major resolving to major
- IV – I cadence (e.g. F – C): subdominant to tonic, both major
- ii – V – I: minor chord to dominant to tonic

**Minor key context:**
- i – bVII – bVI: the descending minor movement (e.g. Am – G – F)
- i – iv: minor tonic to minor subdominant
- V – i: often used as a minor cadence (V can be major even in a minor key)

The presence of a minor i chord and descending movements (i – bVII – bVI – V) strongly suggests minor. The presence of major I, IV, and V chords with no prominent minor chord suggests major.

## Common Traps

**Relative major/minor confusion.** C major and A minor share the same chords. The difference is resolution. Play the progression Am – F – C – G: does it feel like Am is home (minor) or C is home (major)? Listen for where phrases end.

**Borrowed chords.** A song can be in major but borrow the minor iv chord (e.g. C major borrowing Fm). This does not make the song minor. The tonic is still C major.

**Parallel minor.** Some songs switch between major and minor versions of the same root (e.g. A major and A minor) without modulating. This is parallel major/minor motion, not ambiguity — the root stays the same, just the 3rd (and sometimes 6th and 7th) changes.

## Training Your Ear

The simplest ear-training exercise for major/minor:

1. Listen to the first chord of a song. Does it sound settled and bright? Likely major. Settled but darker? Likely minor.
2. Hum the note that feels most like "home" over the music. Then play it on the guitar. Is it the 1st or 3rd of the tonic chord? If the 3rd, adjust.
3. Check whether you feel tension at the end of phrases resolving to that note, or at the beginning of new phrases.

With practice, identifying major vs minor becomes immediate — you do not need to think through the theory. The ear learns to feel the quality of tonal gravity directly.
