## Why Common Progressions Matter

A handful of progressions account for an enormous proportion of popular music. Once you can recognise them instantly — by ear or by Roman numeral — you can navigate a new song without needing to analyse it from scratch. You know the pattern, and you know what it implies.

## The I – V – vi – IV (The Pop Progression)

Perhaps the most common progression in pop music. In C major: C – G – Am – F.

- **Key:** Major
- **Scale:** Major pentatonic or major scale
- **Sound:** Anthemic, hopeful, universally recognisable
- **Variations:** Starts on vi (vi – IV – I – V), starts on IV (IV – I – V – vi)

Examples: *Let It Be* (Beatles), *With or Without You* (U2), *No Woman No Cry* (Marley), *Someone Like You* (Adele), *Frozen* (Let It Go).

## The I – IV – V (The Blues/Rock Standard)

Three major chords on degrees I, IV, and V. In A major: A – D – E.

- **Key:** Major
- **Scale:** Major pentatonic, or minor pentatonic (blues approach)
- **Sound:** Open, driving, fundamental rock and country
- **12-bar variant:** Extends to 12 bars with a turnaround, using dominant 7th versions

Examples: *Johnny B. Goode*, *La Bamba*, *Twist and Shout*, *Wild Thing*.

## The i – bVII – bVI – V (The Minor Descending)

A falling minor-key sequence. In A minor: Am – G – F – E.

- **Key:** Minor (natural minor with a major V for cadential strength)
- **Scale:** Natural minor or minor pentatonic; Aeolian
- **Sound:** Dramatic, descending, melancholic but powerful
- **Variations:** Ends on bVII instead of V for a more open, modern feel

Examples: *Stairway to Heaven* (intro), *All Along the Watchtower*, *Hit the Road Jack*, *Sultans of Swing* (intro).

## The I – vi – IV – V (The 50s Progression)

The classic doo-wop and early rock 'n' roll progression. In C major: C – Am – F – G.

- **Key:** Major
- **Scale:** Major pentatonic or major scale
- **Sound:** Nostalgic, circular, resolves smoothly back to I
- **Note:** vi is a tonic-function chord — the progression is really I – I – IV – V with a minor colour on the I

Examples: *Stand by Me*, *Earth Angel*, *Blue Moon*, *Every Breath You Take*.

## The I – bVII – IV (Mixolydian Loop)

A rock progression that uses the chord one whole step below the tonic. In G: G – F – C.

- **Key:** Mixolydian (or major with borrowed bVII)
- **Scale:** G Mixolydian (or major pentatonic — the b7 note is not in the pentatonic)
- **Sound:** Driving, rock, slightly modal
- **Note:** The F chord (bVII in G) is what creates the Mixolydian flavour

Examples: *Sweet Home Alabama*, *Hey Jude* (coda), *La Grange*, *All Day and All of the Night*.

## The 12-Bar Blues

The defining structure of blues. In A: A7 – A7 – A7 – A7 – D7 – D7 – A7 – A7 – E7 – D7 – A7 – E7.

- **Key:** A (I = A7, IV = D7, V = E7)
- **Scale:** A minor pentatonic or blues scale over all three chords
- **Sound:** The foundation of blues, rock, and much of jazz
- **Note:** All three chords are dominant 7ths — this is unusual in major keys but defines blues harmony

The minor pentatonic works over all three chords because the blues scale approach treats the key root as the only tonal centre and the scale's notes as colour regardless of which chord is playing.

## Quick-Reference Table

| Roman numerals | Common keys | Scale | Genre |
|---------------|-------------|-------|-------|
| I – V – vi – IV | C, G, D, A | Major pentatonic | Pop |
| I – IV – V | A, E, G | Major or minor pentatonic | Rock, country |
| i – bVII – bVI – V | Am, Em, Dm | Natural minor | Rock, ballads |
| I – vi – IV – V | C, G | Major pentatonic | Pop, 50s rock |
| I – bVII – IV | G, D, A | Mixolydian | Classic rock |
| I7 – IV7 – V7 | A, E, G | Minor pentatonic, blues scale | Blues |

Once these are part of your muscle memory, song analysis goes from a laborious exercise to an immediate recognition — you hear four bars and already know the key, the scale, and the chord functions.
