## From Chords to Functions

Once you know the key and quality, replace every chord name with its Roman numeral. This is the most powerful step in the analysis: it reveals the *function* of each chord, which is the same in every key.

**In G major:** G=I, Am=ii, Bm=iii, C=IV, D=V, Em=vi, F#°=vii°

**In E major:** E=I, F#m=ii, G#m=iii, A=IV, B=V, C#m=vi, D#°=vii°

The progressions I–V–vi–IV and I–IV–V–I look identical in both keys. Understanding them as Roman numeral functions means you can recognize, transpose, and analyze them regardless of key.

## The Three Harmonic Functions

Every diatonic chord has one of three functions:

**Tonic function** — stable, at rest. The home chord and its close relatives.
- Major key: I, iii, vi
- Minor key: i, bIII, bVI

**Subdominant function** — moving away from home, creating mild tension.
- Major key: IV, ii
- Minor key: iv, ii°

**Dominant function** — strong tension pulling back to tonic.
- Major key: V, vii°
- Minor key: V (often major, even in minor keys), vii°

A typical phrase moves: tonic → subdominant → dominant → tonic. This is the harmonic engine of most Western music.

## Working Through an Example

Song: *Let It Be* (Beatles) — key of C major.

Chords: C – G – Am – F

- C = I (tonic)
- G = V (dominant)
- Am = vi (tonic function — close relative of I)
- F = IV (subdominant)

Roman numerals: I – V – vi – IV

This is one of the most common progressions in pop music. Recognizing it as I–V–vi–IV rather than "C–G–Am–F" lets you instantly play it in any key and recognize it in any song.

## Non-Diatonic Chords

Most pop and rock songs contain at least one chord that does not belong to the key's diatonic set. Common examples:

**Borrowed chords** — chords taken from the parallel minor or major key.
- E.g. in C major: bVII (Bb major), bVI (Ab major), iv (Fm)
- These chords are not strange or wrong — they add color and are extremely common.

**Secondary dominants** — a V chord applied to a chord other than I.
- E.g. V/V (the V chord of the V chord) — in C major: A major (which is the V of D, the ii chord)
- Sounds like a brief tonicisation — the music hints at a new key without fully modulating.

**Modal mixture** — brief use of chords from a different mode while remaining in the same tonic.

When you encounter a chord that does not fit the key's diatonic set, ask: is it borrowed? Is it a secondary dominant? Or does it signal a key change?

## Labeling in Minor Keys

Minor key analysis uses lowercase Roman numerals for minor chords. The natural minor (Aeolian) diatonic set is:

i – ii° – bIII – iv – v – bVI – bVII

However, in practice, the v chord is often raised to V (major dominant) to create a stronger V – i cadence. This is the harmonic minor influence and is conventional in most Western minor-key music.

Common minor progressions:
- **i – bVII – bVI – V** — the minor scale descending cadence (Am – G – F – E)
- **i – iv – V** — the minor I-IV-V
- **i – bVI – bIII – bVII** — the "Andalusian" or minor loop (Am – F – C – G)

## Practice: Labeling a Song

Choose any song you know. Write out its chords, identify the key and quality, then replace each chord name with its Roman numeral. Check whether all chords are diatonic. If not, identify the non-diatonic chord and determine whether it is borrowed, a secondary dominant, or a signal of modulation.

Do this with three or four songs and the pattern recognition becomes automatic.
