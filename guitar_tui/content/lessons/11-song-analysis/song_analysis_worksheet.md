## Why a Worksheet

The previous lessons taught the method; this one turns it into a routine. When you are listening to a song and want to dig in, the temptation is to jump straight to "what scale do I play?" — question 4 — and skip the three questions that make the answer obvious. The worksheet forces the order.

Copy the template below into a notebook (or any notes app), one copy per song. Fill it in *while listening*, pausing and looping as needed.

## Work Section by Section

Songs are not one thing. Intros often borrow chords from outside the key, bridges are where songwriters modulate, and a chorus can sit in a different mode than its verse. So the worksheet is not one form per song — it is one **header** per song plus one **block per section**. Analyze the section you can hear most clearly first (usually the chorus), not necessarily the one that comes first.

## The Template

```
SONG      ________________________________

ARTIST    ________________________________

TEMPO     ~ ______ BPM          FEEL   straight · swung

SECTIONS  intro · verse · chorus · bridge · ____________


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────


SECTION  ______________


1   TONAL CENTER    ______

    last chord of the loop · bass home note · hummed note


2   QUALITY         major · minor · mode ____________


3   CHORDS          ______   ______   ______   ______   ______

    FUNCTIONS       ______   ______   ______   ______   ______

    outliers        ____________


4   SCALE           ____________________

    target note     ______


NOTES  __________________________________________________


──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
              repeat the section block for each section
```

## How Each Question Meets the App

Work the questions with the tools open — [3] Tools is built for exactly this loop:

1. **Tonal center** — hum the note the song wants to end on, find it on the **Notes on Strings** reference. If two candidates fight, the real one wins the *last* chord of a section.
2. **Quality** — play the tonic chord both ways over the track: the wrong one will tell you immediately. For modal suspects, the **Key View** marks each mode's characteristic note ◆ — hunt for that note in the melody.
3. **Chord functions** — once you have chords but not the key, look a chord up in the **Chord View**: its function list ("vi in C Major · i in A Minor…") is the reverse index. Two or three chords narrow the key to one or two candidates fast. Chords that fit *no* candidate key go in the outliers line — they are usually borrowed, and usually the most interesting thing in the song.
4. **Scale** — enter your answer in the **Song Analysis** tool: key + quality gives you the scale, its positions with fret spans, the diatonic chords to cross-check against question 3, and matching progressions. If the tool's chord list disagrees with what you hear, your answer to question 1 or 2 is wrong — go back, that is the worksheet working.

## Worked Micro-Example

Verse loops **Em – G – D – A**. Tonal center: everything leans back to Em — E. Quality: Em is minor, but a minor key's iv should be Am and here it is A *major*. Check E Dorian in the Key View: i–bIII–bVII–IV = Em–G–D–A. All four fit; the A major chord is the Dorian fingerprint (major IV), and the ◆ characteristic note is C#. Scale: E Dorian, target the C#. Four questions, one conclusion — and the "wrong" chord was the answer.

## Practice

1. Pick a three-chord song you already know and fill in one block. It should take under five minutes — speed comes from the order of the questions, not from talent.
2. Pick a song with a bridge. Fill in a block for verse *and* bridge. If the bridge block comes out identical, you have proven it doesn't modulate — that's a result, not wasted work.
3. Keep every finished worksheet. Ten songs in, patterns emerge: the same functions, the same four progressions, the same borrowed chords. That stack of paper is your own personal Songbook.
