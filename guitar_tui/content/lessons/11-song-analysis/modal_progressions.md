## Tonal vs Modal Harmony

Tonal harmony resolves tension through the V → I cadence. Everything moves toward the tonic, and the V chord creates strong directional pull. Most pop, rock, country, and blues uses tonal harmony.

Modal harmony *doesn't* use this V → I resolution. Instead, it establishes the tonal center through repetition and emphasis — by staying on a chord, by repeating a two-chord loop, or by avoiding the V chord cadence entirely. The music is centered but not driven.

This distinction matters for analysis: in a modal progression, there is no "V" in the functional sense. The Roman numerals describe which scale degrees the chords sit on, but they do not imply the same functional tensions as in tonal harmony.

## Identifying a Modal Progression

Signs that a progression is modal rather than tonal:

1. **No V – I or V7 – I cadence.** The song does not use its dominant chord to resolve home.
2. **The tonic chord is minor.** Minor tonic chords that never hear a V chord pulling them are common in modal music.
3. **Two or three chords loop endlessly.** Many modal songs are built on repeating vamps: i – bVII – i – bVII, or i – IV (Dorian), or i – bII (Phrygian).
4. **The progression uses chords not found in the natural minor or major diatonic set.** Modal chords often include chord types that signal a specific mode.

## The Modal Fingerprints

| Mode | Signature chord | Progression example |
|------|-----------------|---------------------|
| Dorian | iv major (major IV in a minor key) | i – IV (Dm – G) |
| Phrygian | bII major (major chord half-step above tonic) | i – bII (Em – F) |
| Lydian | II major (major chord whole step above major tonic) | I – II (F – G) |
| Mixolydian | bVII major (major chord whole step below major tonic) | I – bVII (G – F) |

If you see a progression that matches one of these patterns and uses the indicated chord as a stable resting point (not a passing chord), the mode name tells you the scale to use.

## Analysis Examples

**Example 1: Dm – G – Dm – G** (repeating)

- Dm is minor (i). G is major. In D natural minor, the IV chord (G) would be minor. But here G is *major*.
- Major IV in a minor key = Dorian. This is D Dorian.
- Scale choice: D Dorian (or D minor pentatonic — the pentatonic is contained within Dorian).

**Example 2: Em – F – Em – F** (repeating)

- Em is minor (i). F is major. F is a half-step above E — this is the bII chord.
- bII major in a minor key = Phrygian. This is E Phrygian.
- Scale choice: E Phrygian. The minor pentatonic works but misses the defining b2 (F).

**Example 3: A – B – A – B** (repeating, both major)

- A is major (I). B is major. B is a whole step above A — this is the II chord.
- II major above a major tonic = Lydian. This is A Lydian.
- Scale choice: A Lydian (A major with #4 = D#/Eb).

**Example 4: D – C – D – C** (repeating, both major)

- D is major (I). C is major. C is a whole step below D — this is the bVII chord.
- bVII major below a major tonic = Mixolydian. This is D Mixolydian.
- Scale choice: D Mixolydian (D major with b7 = C natural).

## When It Is Ambiguous

Many songs sit between tonal and modal. *Hotel California* is in B minor but uses a long chord loop that avoids a conventional V – i resolution — it feels modal but is often analyzed as minor key. The answer is: both analyses are valid, and the scale choice (B natural minor) works either way.

When ambiguous, prioritise what sounds right over what is theoretically clean. The goal of song analysis is to find the tools that let you play well over the music — not to assign a single correct label.

## Practical Summary

When a progression loops on a minor chord:
1. Check whether the IV chord is major (Dorian) or minor (Aeolian).
2. Check whether there is a major chord a half-step above the tonic (Phrygian).
3. If neither, it is likely standard natural minor (Aeolian).

When a progression loops on a major chord:
1. Check whether there is a major chord a whole step *above* (Lydian) or *below* (Mixolydian).
2. If neither, it is standard major (Ionian).
