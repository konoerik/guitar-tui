## What "Being in a Key" Means

A song is in a key when its chords and melody consistently gravitate toward one chord and one note as a resting point. That chord is the tonic (I or i), and the note is the root of the key. Not every song has an obvious key — some are intentionally ambiguous — but most popular music establishes a key within the first few bars.

## Method 1: Find the Chord That Feels Like Home

Listen to the song and identify the chord where the music seems to *land* — where tension resolves, or where the phrase naturally ends. That chord is most likely the tonic.

The verse of a typical song often starts on the tonic or returns to it at the end of each line. The final chord of a song is almost always the tonic. If you can find one chord that the music keeps coming back to as a resting point, you have found the key chord.

The root note of that chord is the key.

## Method 2: Use the Chord Set

If you already know the chords of a song, you can identify the key by finding which major or minor key contains all (or nearly all) of them.

The diatonic chords of a major key are: I ii iii IV V vi vii°

For example, if a song uses G, Em, C, and D:
- G major = I
- Em = vi (relative minor)
- C = IV
- D = V

These are all diatonic to G major. The key is G major.

This works for any key. If you find chords that all belong to one key's diatonic set, that is the key.

## Method 3: Listen for the Resolution

The strongest resolution in Western music is V → I (or V7 → I). If you can identify a moment of strong resolution — a chord that feels like tension releasing into rest — the chord it resolves *to* is the tonic.

In practice: listen for the moment near the end of a section where the music feels "home". The chord there is the I chord. This is particularly clear in songs that end each chorus on the tonic.

## When the Song Modulates

Some songs change key for a section (typically the bridge) and return. Signs of a key change:
- A sudden shift in the emotional colour
- Chords appear that did not fit the original key
- The music lands on a different resting chord

If you notice an unexpected chord, check whether the progression has moved to a new key — often the relative major or minor, or a key a 4th or 5th away.

## Practical Exercise

For any song you want to analyse:

1. Listen through once without thinking — just feel where the music rests.
2. Write down the chords in order (even just approximate names).
3. Try the chord-set method: which key contains these chords?
4. Confirm by checking that the tonic chord (I) feels right at the moments of resolution.

If steps 3 and 4 agree, you have the key. If they conflict, look more carefully — you may have a borrowed chord, a modulation, or a modal progression (covered in the later lessons of this track).
