## The Six Strings

A standard guitar has six strings. In *standard tuning*, from thickest (lowest-pitched) to thinnest (highest-pitched):

| String | Note |
|--------|------|
| 6 | **E** — lowest, thickest |
| 5 | **A** |
| 4 | **D** |
| 3 | **G** |
| 2 | **B** |
| 1 | **e** — highest, thinnest |

A common memory phrase: **"Eddie Ate Dynamite, Good Bye Eddie"** (E–A–D–G–B–E).

These are the *open string* notes — the pitch you hear when you play a string without pressing any fret:

## Frets and Semitones

Each metal strip along the neck is a **fret**. Pressing a string just behind a fret raises the pitch by one **semitone** — the smallest interval in Western music. Moving up one fret = one semitone higher.

Note names cycle through the musical alphabet and then repeat:

`A – A#/Bb – B – C – C#/Db – D – D#/Eb – E – F – F#/Gb – G – G#/Ab – A …`

Two important exceptions: **B–C** and **E–F** are naturally only one semitone apart — there is no sharp or flat between them.

## The 12th Fret and Octaves

After 12 frets the note names repeat, one **octave** higher. The 12th fret of any string sounds the same note as its open string, just higher in pitch. From the 13th fret onward the pattern repeats again.

The natural notes on the low E string — no sharps or flats — are the first landmarks worth memorising:

Frets without a label (2, 4, 6, 9, 11) hold the sharp/flat notes — they exist, but the natural notes are the landmarks to learn first.

## Why It Matters

Every scale pattern and chord shape in this app is built from the notes on this fretboard. Knowing where the natural notes sit — especially on the low `E` and `A` strings — will help you understand why chord shapes and scale positions are placed where they are, and how to move them into other keys.
