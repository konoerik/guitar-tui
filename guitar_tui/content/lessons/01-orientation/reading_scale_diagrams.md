## Scale Diagrams vs Chord Diagrams

Scale diagrams show a *horizontal* section of the fretboard. Where chord diagrams look at a small slice of the neck head-on, scale diagrams look down the neck from above, showing all six strings across a span of frets.

## The Grid

```diagram
type: scale
title: A Minor Pentatonic — Position 1
root: A
fret_range: [5, 8]
positions:
  - {string: 6, fret: 5, degree: "1", root: true}
  - {string: 6, fret: 8, degree: "b3"}
  - {string: 5, fret: 5, degree: "4"}
  - {string: 5, fret: 7, degree: "5"}
  - {string: 4, fret: 5, degree: "b7"}
  - {string: 4, fret: 7, degree: "1", root: true}
  - {string: 3, fret: 5, degree: "b3"}
  - {string: 3, fret: 7, degree: "4"}
  - {string: 2, fret: 5, degree: "5"}
  - {string: 2, fret: 8, degree: "b7"}
  - {string: 1, fret: 5, degree: "1", root: true}
  - {string: 1, fret: 8, degree: "b3"}
```

**Strings** run top to bottom:

| Row | String |
|-----|--------|
| Top | high `e` — thinnest |
| ↓ | `B` |
| ↓ | `G` |
| ↓ | `D` |
| ↓ | `A` |
| Bottom | low `E` — thickest |

**Frets** run left to right, with fret numbers across the top.

## The Two Dot Styles

| Symbol | Meaning |
|--------|---------|
| `●` | A note in the scale |
| `■` | A **root note** — the note the scale is named after |

Root notes are your melodic anchors. Phrases that begin and end on a root note sound resolved. In the diagram above, three `■` markers appear at the 5th fret — those are all `A` notes, the root of the A minor pentatonic.

## The Fret Range

The fret numbers at the top tell you where this shape sits on the neck. The diagram above spans frets 5–8, so every note you play is in that zone. A different position of the same scale appears at a different fret range, but the *shape* of dots follows the same pattern.

## How to Practice from a Scale Diagram

1. Find the lowest root note `■` — usually on the low `E` or `A` string
2. Play each dot from bottom to top, string by string, moving across the rows
3. Come back down in reverse order
4. Once the shape is in your hands, experiment with starting and ending on `■` notes — this is what makes phrases sound musical rather than like a warm-up exercise
