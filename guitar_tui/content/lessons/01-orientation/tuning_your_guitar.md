## The Six Strings

Standard tuning from lowest (thickest) to highest (thinnest):

    6  E  ━━━━━━━━  lowest, thickest string
    5  A
    4  D
    3  G
    2  B
    1  e  ━━━━━━━━  highest, thinnest string

The mnemonic **Eddie Ate Dynamite Good Bye Eddie** gives you the string names in
order from low to high. You will need these names throughout the app.

## Getting in Tune — Two Steps

**Step 1: Set your absolute pitch.** Use a clip-on tuner, a tuner app, or a
piano to get at least one string to a known pitch. The low E string is the
usual starting point. If you have nothing to reference, tune to itself — at
least the strings will be in tune with each other.

**Step 2: Tune string to string using the 5th fret.** Fret a string at the
5th fret — the note you produce should match the pitch of the next open string.
Work your way from low E up to high e.

    String  Fret    Matches open string
    ──────────────────────────────────
    E (6)   5th  →  A (5)
    A (5)   5th  →  D (4)
    D (4)   5th  →  G (3)
    G (3)   4th  →  B (2)   ← 4th fret, not 5th!
    B (2)   5th  →  e (1)

Play the fretted note and the next open string together or alternately and
adjust the open string's tuning peg until the two pitches match. When they
match you will hear the beats (a slow wavering in the sound) slow down and
disappear.

## The G String Exception

Every step above uses the **5th fret** — except one. To match the B string,
fret the G string at the **4th fret**, not the 5th.

This is the most common tuning mistake beginners make. The interval between
G and B is a major third (4 semitones) rather than the perfect fourth
(5 semitones) between every other adjacent string pair. The reason comes from
how standard tuning was designed; for now, just remember: **G string → 4th
fret → B**.

## Quick Sanity Check — 12th Fret Harmonics

Once the strings are tuned, verify each string with a harmonic:

1. Touch the string lightly directly above the 12th fret wire — do not press
   down, just rest your fingertip on the string.
2. Pick the string and immediately lift your finger.
3. The harmonic pitch should match the open string exactly.

If it sounds sharp or flat compared to the open string, the string's intonation
is off — this is a setup issue that requires adjusting the saddle, not the
tuning peg. A well-set-up guitar will pass this check on every string.

## In Practice

Tune before every session. Strings go flat as they warm up and stretch, and
sharper or flatter as temperature and humidity change. A two-minute tuning
check before you start means you are training your ear on accurate pitches
rather than accidentally learning to tolerate out-of-tune sound.

If a chord sounds wrong even though your fingering looks right, check your
tuning before assuming your technique is the problem.
