## The Listening Question

Play E major, then Am. Now play it again. That pull — the E chord *demanding* to resolve to Am — does not exist in natural minor, where the v chord is the mild-mannered Em. Classical composers wanted that pull in minor keys badly enough to change the scale. The result is harmonic minor: natural minor with the 7th raised a half step.

| Scale | Intervals | Notes (key of A) |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| A Natural Minor | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | A B C D E F G |
| A Harmonic Minor | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 **7** | A B C D E F **G#** |

## The Augmented 2nd

Raising G to G# creates something natural minor does not have: a gap of **three half steps** between the b6 and the 7 (F → G#). That interval — the augmented 2nd — is the signature of the scale. Melodies that walk through it sound instantly "exotic" to Western ears: Bach cadences, Yngwie Malmsteen runs, and Django Reinhardt lines all live on this leap.

The augmented 2nd sits between the b6 (string 5, fret 8) and the 7 (string 4, fret 6). Play those two notes back to back — F to G# — and you are playing the sound this whole lesson is about. In the Key View ([3] Tools), select A Harmonic Minor and the 7 is marked ◆ across the whole neck.

## Why It Exists: the Real Dominant

Build a chord on the 5th degree of A natural minor and you get Em (E–G–B) — a minor v with no pull. Build it in harmonic minor and the G# turns it into **E major** (E–G#–B), a true dominant V. The G# is a *leading tone*: one half step below the root, straining upward.

- **i – V – i** (Am – E – Am) — the harmonic minor cadence, the reason the scale exists
- The V is often played as **V7** (E7) for even more pull
- The bIII becomes augmented (C+) — a dramatic, unstable color used sparingly

## Where You Have Heard It

- **Classical** — nearly every minor-key cadence from Bach to Beethoven
- **Neoclassical metal** — Malmsteen, Blackmore; fast runs exploiting the aug 2nd
- **Gypsy jazz** — Django's minor-key vocabulary leans on it constantly
- **Surf** — the opening phrase of "Misirlou" runs its 5th mode (next lesson)

## Practice

1. Play natural minor position 1 (frets 5–8), then harmonic minor position 1. The only change: G (string 4 fret 5) becomes G# (fret 6). Alternate until the difference is automatic.
2. Isolate F → G# → A (string 5 fret 8 → string 4 fret 6 → fret 7). This b6–7–1 climb is the scale's cadential gesture.
3. Loop **Am – E** (two bars each) and improvise: use G natural over Am if you like, but always G# over E. Hear how the G# *is* the E chord's pull.

**Gypsy Cadence Run** in [4] Practice climbs position 1 through the augmented 2nd and lands on the root just as the loop returns to Am.
