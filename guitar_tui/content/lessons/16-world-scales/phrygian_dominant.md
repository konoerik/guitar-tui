## The Listening Question

Strum E major, then F major, then E again. That two-chord rock — a major chord and the major chord one fret above it — is the sound of flamenco, of "Misirlou," of half the Middle Eastern and klezmer repertoire. No other scale produces it. This is Phrygian dominant.

## Two Ways to Build It

**From Phrygian:** take the Phrygian mode (1 b2 b3 4 5 b6 b7) and raise the 3rd. The b2 stays — the darkness stays — but the tonic chord becomes major.

**From harmonic minor:** play A harmonic minor starting from E. Same seven notes, new root:

| Scale | Intervals | Notes (key of E) |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| E Phrygian | 1 b2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | E F G A B C D |
| E Phrygian Dominant | 1 b2 **3** 4 5 b6 b7 | E F **G#** A B C D |

The major 3rd (G#) against the b2 (F) creates the scale's own augmented 2nd — F to G#, one string apart in the open position.

## The Harmony

Build triads on the scale and the fingerprint appears immediately:

- **I is major** (E) — unlike Phrygian's minor i
- **bII is major** (F) — the chord one half step above home
- **I – bII – I** — the Spanish cadence; flamenco's *compás* patterns cycle on it endlessly

Because the I is major and carries a b7 in the scale (D natural), the tonic often behaves like a dominant 7th chord that never resolves — E7 vamping forever. That permanent, unresolved dominant tension *is* the style.

## Where You Have Heard It

- **Flamenco** — the falling Am–G–F–E chord run (the Andalusian world) landing on a major tonic
- **Surf** — the opening phrase of "Misirlou" runs the scale straight up one string
- **Klezmer** — known there as *Freygish*; the standard mode of celebration tunes
- **Metal** — the b2/major-3 combination under heavy distortion

## Practice

1. Play the open-position scale slowly, letting open strings ring. String 3: G# at fret 1 against the F on string 4, fret 3 — hear the augmented 2nd.
2. Vamp **E – F – E** (one bar each). Over it, walk the top string: open E, F at fret 1, back. The half step above the root is home base, not an avoid note.
3. Descend the full scale from the high E string down to the open low E — the flamenco gesture.

**Spanish Descent** in [4] Practice is exactly that: a top-to-bottom cascade through the open position over an E – F vamp.
