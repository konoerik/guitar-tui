## The Listening Question

You know the minor pentatonic — five notes chosen so that *nothing* clashes. Traditional Japanese koto music also uses five notes, but chooses them for the opposite reason. Two of the steps are bare half steps, and those small, exposed movements are what make the sound unmistakable — spare, formal, and instantly evocative.

The most common form on guitar is **hirajoshi**, from a standard koto tuning:

| Scale | Intervals | Notes (key of A) |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| A Minor Pentatonic | 1 b3 4 5 b7 | A C D E G |
| A Hirajoshi | 1 2 **b3** 5 **b6** | A B C E F |

Compare the two: hirajoshi keeps the 1, b3, and 5, but replaces the "safe" 4 and b7 with a 2 and a b6 — creating half steps at **2 → b3** (B→C) and **5 → b6** (E→F).

## The Sound

The two half steps behave like miniature cadences:

- **b6 → 5** (F → E) — a sigh downward onto the scale's most stable non-root tone
- **b3 → 2 → 1** (C → B → A) — a stepwise fall to the root

Notice both gestures resolve *downward*. Where blues pentatonic phrasing pushes up (bends, upward slurs), hirajoshi phrasing releases down. Play the same five notes with upward energy and the effect vanishes — the tradition lives in the direction of movement as much as the notes.

There is no functional chord progression underneath this music historically; a drone on the root serves perfectly.

## Where You Have Heard It

- **Koto and shamisen repertoire** — *Sakura* is essentially this scale
- **Video game scores** — the default cue for Japanese settings since the 8-bit era
- **Metal and fusion** — Marty Friedman built a signature style on these intervals

## Not Every "Eastern" Sound Is a Scale

A framing note for this whole track: musical character is not always scale-driven. Chinese folk melodies largely use the *major* pentatonic you already know — the identity lives in instrumentation and ornament. Bossa nova is jazz harmony with Brazilian rhythm, not an exotic scale. When you chase a sound, ask first whether it lives in the notes, the rhythm, or the timbre. This track covered the sounds that genuinely live in the notes.

## Practice

1. Play the box slowly, letting every note ring fully into the next — koto phrasing, not blues phrasing.
2. Isolate the two falls: F → E (string 5, fret 8 → 7) and C → B → A (string 6, fret 8 → 7 → 5). End every phrase with one of them.
3. Loop a low **Am** (or just a bare A note) as a drone and improvise with maximum space — count a full beat of silence between phrases.
