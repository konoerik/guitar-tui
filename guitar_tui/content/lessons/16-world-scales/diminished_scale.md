## The Listening Question

The tremolo chord under a silent-film villain; the spiraling run in a bebop solo just before the resolution; the lurch in a horror score when something moves in the dark. All one scale — the diminished scale, built by strictly alternating **whole and half steps**:

| Scale | Intervals | Notes (key of C) |
|-------|-----------|------------------|
| C Diminished (W–H) | 1 2 b3 4 b5 b6 6 7 | C D Eb F Gb Ab A B |

Eight notes — one more than any scale you have learned. Like the whole tone scale it is **symmetric**, this time repeating every minor 3rd (3 frets): C, Eb, Gb, and A diminished scales are all the same eight notes.

## The Chord Connection

Take every other note of the scale: C – Eb – Gb – A. That is a **diminished 7th chord** — the fully symmetric chord you met in the chord library, the one that repeats every 3 frets in moveable shapes. The W–H diminished scale is that chord with passing tones filled in; chord and scale share the same 3-fret symmetry, which is why both move up the neck in minor-3rd steps.

Jazz players also use the scale's sibling — starting half-whole instead — over dominant 7th chords (the "dominant diminished" sound: b9, #9, and 13 all available). Same eight-note machinery, offset by one note.

## Where You Have Heard It

- **Jazz** — tension lines over dim7 and altered dominant chords, resolving on the next downbeat
- **Horror and thriller scores** — the dim7 tremolo *is* the genre
- **Classical** — diminished 7th chords drove dramatic modulations for two centuries
- **Progressive metal** — symmetric runs that ladder the neck in 3-fret jumps

## Practice

1. Play position 1, then the same shape at frets 5–8 and 8–11. Same scale, three addresses. This is the fastest neck-covering pattern you will ever learn.
2. Arpeggiate C° (C–Eb–Gb–A) from the chord library, then fill in the scale around it. Hear the chord as the scale's skeleton.
3. Loop **C°** and play rising four-note cells (C–D–Eb–F, then Eb–F–Gb–Ab…). Symmetric scales love sequences — each cell repeats its neighbor's shape.
