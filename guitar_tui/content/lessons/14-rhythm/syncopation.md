## Strong Beats, Weak Beats

In 4/4 time the ear expects weight on the numbers — most strongly on 1 and 3. **Syncopation** deliberately shifts that weight to the slots the ear treats as weak: the "&"s between beats, or beats 2 and 4. The expectation is violated, the ear leans forward, and the result is what musicians call *groove*.

Nothing about syncopation is fast. Every example in this lesson uses plain eighth notes you already play. Only the *placement of emphasis* changes.

## The Backbeat

The foundation of rock, blues, and pop: beats 2 and 4 carry the accent. Drummers put the snare there; as a rhythm guitarist you match it by strumming 2 and 4 harder — or by strumming *only* 2 and 4 while the band covers the rest.

## Off-Beat Strumming

Now remove the downbeats entirely. Strumming only the "&"s is the sound of reggae and ska guitar — and the best exercise there is for internalizing where the off-beats live.

This will feel wrong at first — your hand wants to land on the numbers. That discomfort is the exercise. When the off-beats start to feel like home, you own the eighth-note grid from both sides.

## The 3-3-2 Pattern

The most widespread syncopated figure in popular music divides eight eighth-note slots into groups of 3 + 3 + 2: hits on beat 1, the "&" of 2, and beat 4. It drives Latin music (as the *tresillo*), and countless pop, rock, and EDM songs.

```diagram
type: tab
title: 3-3-2 — A5
time: "4/4"
caption: "Hits on 1, the '&' of 2, and 4. Count all eight slots aloud (1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &) and let each chord ring until the next hit."
lines:
  - beats:
      - notes: [5, 7, null, null, null, null]
        duration: 3
        label: "1"
      - notes: [5, 7, null, null, null, null]
        duration: 3
        label: "&2"
      - notes: [5, 7, null, null, null, null]
        duration: 2
        label: "4"
```

The second hit is the syncopation: it lands between beats 2 and 3 and *carries through* beat 3 without restriking. Letting a note ring across a strong beat is called an anticipation — the chord arrives early and the ear feels the pull.

## Common Mistakes

- **Turning off-beats into downbeats** — after a few bars of the off-beat exercise, players unconsciously drift until their upstrokes land on the numbers again. Keep the metronome on and treat every click as a *silent* moment.
- **Rushing the 3-3-2** — the third hit (beat 4) tends to arrive early. Count "1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &" out loud; the hit pattern is "**1** … **&** … **4**".
- **Accenting with the whole arm** — a backbeat accent is a slightly faster wrist snap, not a bigger swing. The tempo of the hand does not change, only the attack.
