## Where Solos Actually Go Wrong

Record yourself improvising and listen with fresh ears. The wrong notes you feared are probably rare. What you will actually hear: phrases that start vaguely *near* a beat, note values that drift between eighths and nothing in particular, endings that evaporate. **Lead guitar is rhythm played one note at a time** — and every tool from this track applies directly.

The discipline is the same one strumming taught you: know the grid you are on, know which slot each note occupies.

## The Three Transfers

Everything from the strumming lessons transfers to single notes:

1. **Subdivision awareness** → note values. Decide, per phrase, whether you are speaking in quarters, eighths, or sixteenths — and know when you switch. A phrase that wanders between grids sounds unsure; a phrase that shifts grids *deliberately* (eighths building to sixteenths at the peak) sounds like an arc.
2. **Syncopation** → anticipations and off-beat entries. A note struck on the "&" and held across the beat pulls a lead line forward exactly as it pulls a strummed chord.
3. **The constant-motion rule** → alternate picking. Down-strokes on the numbers and "&"s, up-strokes on the "e"s and "a"s, whether or not a note sounds there. Your picking hand should always know what slot it is in — that knowledge *is* your time.

## Space Is a Rhythmic Event

A rest in a lead line has a duration, exactly like a note. "Rest for three eighth-slots, enter on the '&' of 2" is a rhythmic decision; "stop for a bit" is not. Players with great time treat their silences as strictly as their notes — you practiced this in the phrasing track from the melodic side; here it is the rhythmic side of the same coin.

## Locking to the Groove

Lead lines do not float above the rhythm section — they interlock with it. Three habits that build the lock:

- **Tap your foot on 2 and 4** (the backbeat) rather than 1 and 3 while soloing. It is harder and it wires you to the snare.
- **Sing the rhythm before playing it.** If you can vocalize a phrase's rhythm ("da — da-da — daa"), your hands will place it; if you cannot, no amount of scale knowledge will.
- **Practice leads with a metronome on the off-beats only.** Set it to half your tempo and hear the clicks as the "&"s. Brutal at first; transformative after.

## Practice

1. **One pitch, all rhythm**: solo for a full loop cycle on the note A alone (fret 5, low E — and its octaves if you must). With melody removed, every ounce of interest must come from placement, duration, and space. This is the single best rhythm-in-leads exercise there is.
2. **Grid switching**: improvise a phrase in eighths, repeat it in sixteenths at the same tempo, return to eighths. Metronome on.
3. Work the two licks below — the *3-3-2 Riff* and the *Displaced Cell* — until you can move their placements to other notes on demand.

## Common Mistakes

- **Practicing leads without a click** — scales get metronome discipline, solos somehow don't. Reverse that: improvisation is where the metronome earns its keep.
- **Speed as a substitute for placement** — fast vague notes are still vague. A quarter note in the pocket beats a flurry beside it.
- **Ignoring the last note** — the final note of a phrase carries its rhythm home. Give it a slot and a length on purpose.
