## The Beat Is a Container

A beat is not a single point in time — it is a container you can divide. Play four beats at 80 BPM and you can fill each one with one strum, two strums, or four strums without changing the tempo at all. Those three ways of filling the beat are the **subdivisions**: quarter notes, eighth notes, and sixteenth notes.

Most rhythm problems are not speed problems. They are *awareness* problems — not knowing which subdivision your hand is supposed to be tracking. This lesson builds that awareness with one chord so your full attention goes to time.

All three diagrams below cover the same amount of musical time: one bar of 4/4. Notice how the strums pack tighter while the bar stays the same width.

## The Switching Drill

Knowing each grid is not enough — the skill is *moving between them without the tempo drifting*. Set the metronome to 70 BPM and cycle:

1. One bar of quarters (4 strums)
2. One bar of eighths (8 strums)
3. One bar of sixteenths (16 strums)
4. Back down: eighths, then quarters

The metronome click never changes. Only your hand density changes. If the click seems to speed up when you switch to sixteenths, you are rushing — this is the single most common timing fault, and this drill is the cure.

## Common Mistakes

- **Rushing the smaller subdivisions** — sixteenths feel busy, so the hands push ahead. Count out loud; the voice disciplines the hands.
- **Stopping the hand between strums** — at eighths and sixteenths the strumming hand should swing continuously like a pendulum, even through beats it does not strike. A moving hand always knows where the next subdivision is.
- **Tension creeping in at sixteenths** — the motion should come from the wrist, small and loose. If your forearm burns after one bar, you are strumming from the elbow.

## What's Next

Every strumming pattern you have played — and every one you will learn — is a selection of slots from one of these grids. The next lesson uses that idea directly: syncopation is what happens when the *emphasized* slots are not the ones the ear expects.
