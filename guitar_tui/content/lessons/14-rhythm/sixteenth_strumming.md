## The Constant-Motion Rule

On the sixteenth grid the strumming hand moves down-up twice per beat, every beat, without exception: **D** on the number, **U** on the "e", **D** on the "&", **U** on the "a". Sixteen passes per bar.

A *pattern* is not a new hand motion. It is a decision about which of those sixteen passes make contact with the strings. The passes that miss are not wasted — they are what keeps your time locked. This is the single most important idea in strumming technique: **the hand is a metronome; the pattern is a filter.**

Every diagram below keeps the count in the caption. The `r` slots are misses — the hand still travels, it just doesn't touch.

## Pattern 2: The Acoustic Pop Groove

The workhorse of modern acoustic playing — half a bar of the pattern is **D · · U · U D ·**, played twice. Nine strums out of sixteen passes.

Check the D/U logic against the constant-motion rule: the "a" of beat 1 is always an up-pass, so its strum is a U. The "&" of beat 2 is always a down-pass, so its strum is a D. You never decide the direction — the grid decides it for you.

## Pattern 3: Funk Scratch

Funk rhythm keeps the full sixteen-pass motion but chokes most passes with a relaxed fret hand (lay fingers across the strings without pressing). The choked passes produce a percussive *chk* — shown here as rests; in practice they are muted scratches, not silence.

## Practicing the Patterns

- **Tempo**: 60–70 BPM. Sixteenths at 70 are faster than they sound — resist the urge to start higher.
- **Count aloud**: "1 e & a" spoken out loud while playing is uncomfortable and enormously effective. If you cannot say it, you cannot feel it.
- **One chord first**: pattern practice with chord changes is two exercises at once. Stay on Am until the pattern runs itself, then add a change (Am–F or Am–G) at the bar line.

## Common Mistakes

- **Stopping the hand on misses** — the instant the hand pauses, the next strum's direction becomes a guess. Constant motion is non-negotiable.
- **Big motion** — sixteen passes per bar from the elbow is exhausting and slow. The motion is a loose wrist rotation covering only the strings you mean to hit.
- **Losing the grid on the scratch pattern** — funk scratching hides timing drift under noise. Record yourself against the metronome; the *chk*s should land exactly on the grid.

## What's Next

The last core rhythm skill is textural: palm muting, which controls not *when* the strings sound but *how* — the difference between an open ringing chord and the tight chug that drives rock rhythm.
