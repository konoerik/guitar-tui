## The Third State

So far every slot in a strumming pattern has had two possible states: strummed or silent. Ghost strokes add the third: the hand strikes the strings, but the fretting hand has released pressure — fingers still touching, no longer pressing — so the strike produces a pitchless percussive *chk*.

In these diagrams, ghost strokes are written as rests with a `x` label; the notation note is important: **an `r` slot marked `x` is struck, not skipped**. The caption of each diagram says which.

## The Mechanics

1. Fret an A minor chord normally.
2. Without moving any finger, relax the pressure until the strings lift off the frets. Fingers stay in contact — they are now dampers.
3. Strum. That *chk* is the ghost stroke.
4. Re-press. The chord is instantly back.

The skill is the release-press cycle without repositioning: pressure changes, shape does not. Work the switch slowly — chord, ghost, chord, ghost — before putting it in time.

## Sixteenth-Note Ghosting

On the sixteenth grid, ghosting is the core of funk rhythm (you met its cousin, the scratch, in the 16th-strumming lesson). The full texture: accented chords surface out of a continuous bed of muted sixteenths.

## Ghosts in the Fret Hand's Absence

Open chords complicate ghosting: open strings have no finger to release. Two options guitarists use constantly:

- **Flatten a spare finger** (or the underside of the barring finger) lightly across all six strings for the ghost slots.
- **Palm-edge assist**: for low-string patterns, a quick palm-mute touch from the picking hand does the damping (Track 14's palm-muting lesson, in miniature).

Barre chords ghost most easily of all — release the barre's pressure and everything mutes at once. This is why funk rhythm lives on barre shapes.

## Common Mistakes

- **Lifting fingers off the strings** — the fingers must stay touching; lifted fingers mean ringing open strings instead of a *chk*.
- **Ghosts louder than chords** — the ghost is texture, not accent. If the *chk* dominates, lighten the strike.
- **Losing pitch on the chords** — releasing too slowly (or re-pressing too late) makes the real chords bloom instead of speak. The pressure switch must be instant.
- **Treating ghosts as optional decoration** — practice them as strictly as notes. A dragged ghost drags the groove exactly like a dragged chord.
