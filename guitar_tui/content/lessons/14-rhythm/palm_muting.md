## What Palm Muting Is

Rest the fleshy edge of your picking hand — the side below the little finger — on the strings *right where they meet the bridge*, and keep it there while you pick. The strings still sound, but shortened and thickened: a tight *chug* instead of an open ring.

Tab has no dedicated symbol for it in this app; diagrams mark it in the caption (the standard notation elsewhere is "P.M." with a dashed line). The frets and rhythm read exactly as normal.

## Placement Is Everything

The technique lives or dies on where the hand sits:

- **On the bridge itself** — no muting at all; the strings ring normally. (Useful to know: this is the "home" position many players rest in.)
- **Just in front of the bridge (1–2 cm)** — the palm-mute zone. Notes keep their pitch but lose sustain. This is the target.
- **Too far forward (over the pickups / soundhole)** — the pitch chokes into a dead thud. If your chug has no note in it, slide the hand back toward the bridge.

Pressure matters as much as position: light contact. Pressing hard bends the strings sharp and kills the note entirely.

## Degrees of Muting

Once the on/off switch is solid, explore the range between. Sliding the palm slightly forward increases the damping; easing pressure lightens it. Players use this continuously — heavier muting in verses, lighter as a chorus approaches. Think of it as a tone control operated by your picking hand.

## Common Mistakes

- **Hand too far from the bridge** — the most common fault by far. If the pitch disappears, move back.
- **Muting with pick-hand fingers instead of the palm edge** — the mute must come from the part of the hand that stays planted while the pick moves.
- **Losing the mute during string changes** — moving from the low E to the A string drags the palm off position. Keep the anchor point fixed and let the wrist reach.
- **Uneven chugs** — record one bar; every muted stroke should be a clone of the last. Unevenness here is a timing problem wearing a tone-problem costume.
