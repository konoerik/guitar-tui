## The Shape

The A-shape minor barre derives from the open Am chord. Compared to the A-shape major, string 2 drops by one fret — the major 3rd becomes a minor 3rd, the same one-note change that distinguishes all major/minor pairs.

## Fingering

Unlike the A-shape major (where strings 4, 3, 2 are at the same fret), the A-shape minor places string 2 one fret lower. This breaks up the mini-barre option — you need individual fingers:

- **Index (1)**: barre strings 1–5 at base fret
- **Ring (3)**: string 4, two frets above barre
- **Pinky (4)**: string 3, two frets above barre
- **Middle (2)**: string 2, one fret above barre
- Strings 6 (muted), 5, and 1 at barre level

Middle finger on the B string (one fret above barre) while ring and pinky hold strings 4 and 3 two frets up is a wider stretch than the major shape. Take time to position each finger carefully.

## Completing the System

With all four shapes — E-shape major, E-shape minor, A-shape major, A-shape minor — you can play any major or minor chord in any position on the neck. Two roots (strings 5 and 6), two chord qualities (major and minor), four shapes. Every chromatic key is covered twice: once with the root on string 6 and once with the root on string 5.

| Chord | E-shape (string 6 root) | A-shape (string 5 root) |
|-------|------------------------|------------------------|
| G major | fret 3 | fret 10 |
| G minor | fret 3 | fret 10 |
| D major | fret 10 | fret 5 |
| D minor | fret 10 | fret 5 |

Choosing which position to use comes down to where you are on the neck and what chord comes next — minimise the distance your hand has to travel between chord changes.
