## The Shape

The E-shape major barre is built from the open E major chord. The index finger barres all six strings; the remaining fingers form the familiar E major voicing above.

## Reading the Neck for Root Position

The root of the E-shape major barre sits on string 6 (low E). To play any major chord with this shape, place your index finger at the fret where that chord's root falls on the low E string:

| Root on low E | Fret | Chord |
|--------------|------|-------|
| F | 1 | F major |
| F# | 2 | F# major |
| G | 3 | G major |
| Ab | 4 | Ab major |
| A | 5 | A major |
| Bb | 6 | Bb major |
| B | 7 | B major |
| C | 8 | C major |
| C# | 9 | C# major |
| D | 10 | D major |
| Eb | 11 | Eb major |

Memorising the note names on the low E string is the key skill — once you know where each note lives, you can play any major chord in any key instantly.

## Fingering Detail

- **Index finger (1)**: barres all six strings at the base fret. Press close to the fret wire for a clean sound. The fingertip rolls slightly toward the nut to use the bony edge of the finger, which presses more effectively than the fleshy pad.
- **Ring finger (3)**: string 5 (A), two frets above barre. Acts as the root confirmation on the A string (the same root, one octave lower than string 1).
- **Pinky (4)**: string 4 (D), two frets above barre. The 5th of the chord.
- **Middle finger (2)**: string 3 (G), one fret above barre. The major 3rd.
- Strings 2 and 1 are covered by the barre at the base fret.

## Common Difficulties

**Buzzing on strings 1 or 2**: the index finger is not pressing flat enough, or the joint fold is landing on one of those strings. Roll the finger slightly toward the nut to flatten the pressing surface.

**Muted string 5**: the ring finger tip is accidentally touching string 5. Curve the ring finger more sharply so only the tip contacts string 5.

**Weak bass note**: the index finger is too far from the fret wire. Move it as close to the fret as possible on the bass-side strings.

## Practical Use

The E-shape major barre fills the gap in keys that have no open chord option. In a song using G–Bb–C–F (common in rock and pop), three of the four chords are only practical as E-shape barres: Bb (fret 6), C (fret 8), F (fret 1). G can be played open. The E-shape barre is the tool that makes this progression physically possible.
