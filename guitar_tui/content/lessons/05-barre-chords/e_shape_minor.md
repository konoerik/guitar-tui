## One Note Difference

The E-shape minor barre is built from the open Em chord. Compared to the E-shape major, the only change is removing the middle finger — the major 3rd on string 3 drops back to the barre level, becoming a minor 3rd.

## Root Positions on String 6

Same principle as the major shape — the root sits on string 6 at the barre fret:

| Root on low E | Fret | Chord |
|--------------|------|-------|
| Fm | 1 | F minor |
| F#m | 2 | F# minor |
| Gm | 3 | G minor |
| G#m | 4 | G# minor |
| Am | 5 | A minor |
| Bbm | 6 | Bb minor |
| Bm | 7 | B minor |
| Cm | 8 | C minor |
| C#m | 9 | C# minor |
| Dm | 10 | D minor |
| Ebm | 11 | Eb minor |

## Fingering

- **Index finger (1)**: full six-string barre at base fret.
- **Ring finger (3)**: string 5, two frets above barre.
- **Pinky (4)**: string 4, two frets above barre.
- Strings 3, 2, and 1 are all covered by the barre.

With only two fingers above the barre (ring and pinky), the minor shape is physically simpler than the major shape in one sense — but the index finger now needs to cleanly fret strings 3, 2, and 1 simultaneously, which requires even pressure across the barre.

## Switching Between Major and Minor

Because the shapes differ by only one finger, switching between E-shape major and minor at the same position is fast. This makes it useful for progressions that move between parallel major and minor (for example, G major → G minor → Bb major) where repositioning to a different chord would be slower.

## Practical Context

The E-shape minor barre fills all the minor chord positions that have no open equivalent — F#m, G#m, Bbm, Cm, C#m, Ebm. In the key of A (A–D–E–F#m), F#m is only practical as an E-shape minor barre at fret 2. In a song using the I–V–vi–IV progression in A, the vi chord (F#m) is the one that requires barre technique.

## What's Next
