## The Principle: Minimize Movement

With barre chords, chord changes can happen on the same string set or across string sets. The most efficient approach: identify whether two chords share a root string, and choose the voicing that requires the least hand travel.

**Example**: moving from G major to C major:
- G E-shape (fret 3) → C E-shape (fret 8): move 5 frets along the same string
- G E-shape (fret 3) → C A-shape (fret 3): stay at the same fret, change strings

The second option (same fret, change shape from E to A) is faster and requires no position shift — just a shape change.

## The I–V–vi–IV as Barres (Key of B)

B major is awkward to play in open position but natural with barres:

| Chord | Roman | Shape | Fret |
|-------|-------|-------|------|
| B | I | E-shape | 7 |
| F# | V | E-shape | 2 |
| G#m | vi | E-shape | 4 |
| E | IV | E-shape | 0 (open or barre 12) |

Three of the four can be played as E-shape barres; E can be played open. This demonstrates how barre knowledge extends your accessible key range dramatically.

## Building Fluency

Barre chord changes improve through deliberate repetition of specific transitions:

1. **Isolate the hard change**: in a four-chord progression, identify the one change that feels worst and loop just that pair (from the last beat of chord 1 to the first beat of chord 2).
2. **Land on time, not on pitch first**: aim to have your hand in position by beat 1, even if the chord does not ring cleanly yet. Timing before tone.
3. **Use a metronome at half speed**: reduce the tempo until the change is effortless, then increase by 5 bpm at a time.
4. **Same-fret changes last**: practice moving along string 6 (all E-shapes in one key) before mixing E-shape and A-shape changes.
