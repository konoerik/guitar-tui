## What Is CAGED?

CAGED stands for **C – A – G – E – D** — the five open chord shapes that together tile the entire guitar neck. Every major chord has five distinct positions across the fretboard, each corresponding to one of these five shapes. The shapes interlock sequentially, repeating the cycle every 12 frets.

You have already used two of these shapes:
- **E-shape**: the foundation of E-shape major and minor barre chords
- **A-shape**: the foundation of A-shape major and minor barre chords

The remaining three (C, G, D) are less commonly taught as barre chords but exist at every position and are used for chord inversions, partial chord grips, and lead guitar context.

## The Interlocking Sequence

The five shapes connect in a fixed order: **C – A – G – E – D – C – A – G …** Each shape's highest chord tones connect to the next shape's lowest chord tones — the top of one position overlaps with the bottom of the next.

For C major: the C-shape is at frets 1–3, the A-shape picks up at frets 3–5, the G-shape at 5–7, the E-shape at 7–10, the D-shape at 10–12, then the C-shape repeats at 12–15.

## Why CAGED Matters for Lead Guitar

The CAGED system is most powerful not just for chord voicings but for understanding *scale positions*. The five positions of any pentatonic scale, major scale, or mode align with the five CAGED shapes. A scale position labelled "E-shape" in the scale diagrams in this app corresponds to the fret region where the E-shape chord for that key lives.

This means: when you are soloing over an A major chord and the chord player is using an A-shape voicing, you can use the A-shape scale position in the same fret region — your notes will directly reinforce the chord tones because they are drawn from the same position of the neck.

## Practical Starting Point

You do not need to learn all five CAGED shapes immediately. Start with the two you already have (E and A) and add the others gradually:

1. **C-shape**: the familiar open C chord, used as a partial barre or without the root on string 6
2. **G-shape**: the open G chord — involves a four-fret stretch, less common as a full barre
3. **D-shape**: the open D chord shape moved up the neck, primarily used for high-register chord fragments

Each new shape you add extends your fretboard vocabulary and connects another region of the neck to the musical concepts you already understand.
