## The Sharpest Tool on the Ladder

The major–minor switch borrowed a scale for a phrase. This rung borrows **one
note, for one chord, for as long as that chord lasts** — and then gives it
back. It is the most precise move in this track, and the gateway to how
advanced players think about every chord.

The situation: you are soloing in A minor, and the progression contains an
**E or E7 chord** — the major V. Minor-key songs do this constantly (the
Sound of Harmonic Minor lesson explains why: the raised 7th turns the weak Em
into a true dominant that pulls back to Am). The chord contains **G#**. Your
scale contains G. For the length of that chord, the chord wins.

## The Substitution

> Over everything else: **G** (fret 5, D string). Over the E chord: **G#**
> (fret 6, D string). That is the entire technique.

While G# sounds, you are momentarily playing A harmonic minor — but thinking
"harmonic minor scale" is heavier machinery than the moment needs. Think
instead: *the E chord's own 3rd is G#, and I am agreeing with the chord.* The
note is not a color you chose; it is a fact of the harmony you acknowledged.

Two things make the move land:

- **Commit.** A timid G# sounds like a missed G. Give it duration, vibrato,
  a bend into it — the note is supposed to blaze.
- **Resolve.** G# is a leading tone, a half step below A. Its entire purpose
  is to arrive. Release it onto A (or onto the E chord's root E) and the
  tension pays off; abandon it and the phrase dangles.

## Why This Rung Matters Beyond Itself

Notice what you just did: you looked at one chord in the progression, found
the note where it disagrees with your scale, and played the chord's version.
That is **chord-tone targeting** — the skill that separates players who play
*over* songs from players who play *the songs*. You have now done it in
miniature. The final lesson does it across an entire progression.

## Practice

1. Loop **Am – E**, two bars each (the i–V cadence — Key View in [3] Tools
   has it under A Harmonic Minor). Solo normally over Am; over E, find and
   hold the G# every single pass until the reach to fret 6 is automatic.
2. Practice the resolution specifically: G# held through the end of the E
   bars, landing on A (D string, fret 7) exactly on the Am downbeat. Early
   and the tension fizzles; late and the clash lingers. The timing *is* the
   technique.
3. Deliberately play G natural over the E chord once. That mild wrongness —
   the b3 grinding against the chord's major 3rd — is what the substitution
   fixes. (Blues gets away with that grind on purpose; a minor-key cadence
   wants it resolved.)
4. **The G# Spotlight** in [4] Practice isolates the move; **Gypsy Cadence
   Run** from the World Scales track puts it inside a full harmonic minor
   line once the single note feels easy.
