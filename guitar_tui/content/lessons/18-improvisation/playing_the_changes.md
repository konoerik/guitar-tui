## One Progression, Every Rung

Here is a six-chord minor-key progression (they happen to be the traditional
changes of "House of the Rising Sun," a song the Songbook analyzes in full):

> **Am – C – D – F – Am – E – Am**

Look at it with the ladder's eyes and something remarkable appears: this one
progression contains *every decision this track taught*. There is a major IV
(D) voting for F#, an F chord voting for F natural — **both sixths inside the
same eight bars** — a parent-major moment (C), and a major V (E) demanding its
G#. A single scale cannot satisfy this progression. That is exactly why it is
the perfect final exam.

## The Map

For each chord, one question: *which of my notes does this chord care about?*

| Chord | Spelled | The note that matters | Which rung |
|-------|---------|----------------------|------------|
| Am | A–C–E | home — everything from the ladder works | — |
| C | C–E–G | land C or E — the parent-major moment, pure pentatonic | 2 |
| D | D–F#–A | **F#** (B string, fret 7) — the chords voted Dorian for one bar | 3 |
| F | F–A–C | **F natural** (B string, fret 6) — the same vote, reversed, one bar later | 2, 3 |
| E | E–G#–B | **G#** (D string, fret 6) — the harmonic minor moment | 5 |

The D-then-F pair is the progression's gift to your ears: fret 7, then fret 6,
on the same string, one bar apart. No song could demonstrate "the chords
decide" more literally.

This skeleton is not yet music — it is the *load-bearing structure* of music.
Real phrases drape pentatonic motion, bends, and rhythm over these targets:
approach the F# from E below, fall onto the F from G above, trill around the
G# before releasing it. The targets stay; everything else is decoration.

## The Method, Named

What you just did, in order: identified the key, spelled each chord, found
where each chord agrees or disagrees with the key's scale, and aimed your
phrases at those notes. That is the whole method, and it transfers to any
song — the Four Questions from the Song Analysis track are exactly this
process formalized, and the Song Analysis Worksheet gives you a place to run
it. The ladder was never really about A minor.

## Practice

1. Loop the full progression, one bar per chord, slowly. First pass: whole
   notes only — the target line above, nothing else. Let each note ring
   against its chord and *listen for the agreement*.
2. Second pass: same targets on the downbeats, pentatonic motion between
   them. The targets are rocks in the stream; the pentatonic is the water.
3. Third pass: full ladder. B and F as colors over Am, the switch of feel
   over C, F# spotlighted over D, F over F, G# blazing over E and resolving
   home. Record it. Compare it with a one-scale solo over the same loop from
   before this track. That difference is what you built.
4. **Running the Changes** in [4] Practice is the target line as a lick —
   loop it until the map is under your fingers, then abandon it for your own.
