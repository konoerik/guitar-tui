## The Plateau

You know the minor pentatonic box, probably in more than one position. You can
solo over a backing track and nothing sounds wrong. And after a while, nothing
sounds surprising either — every phrase draws from the same five notes, and the
ear starts predicting them.

The way forward is not a pile of new scales to memorize. It is a short ladder
of *single-note additions*, each one changing the sound more than its size
suggests. This track climbs that ladder one rung per lesson, and stays in
**A minor the whole way** — same key, same box, so the only thing changing is
the notes you add.

## The Reframe

Here is the idea the whole track builds toward: the useful question is not
*"which scale do I switch to?"* but *"which chord is happening right now?"*
The chords underneath decide which added notes shine and which ones clash.
By the last lesson you will choose notes chord by chord. For now, one
principle unlocks the first rung.

Ask yourself why that note works. D# against an Am chord is, on paper, one of
the harshest sounds available. Yet every blues player uses it constantly —
because of *how* it is used. You pass through it, bend through it, slide off
it. You do not sit on it.

## The Rule of the Track

That is the principle every rung of this ladder runs on:

> **A new note is not simply "in" or "out." It is either a *color* — safe to
> land on and hold — or a *tension* — a note you pass through or resolve.
> Treatment matters more than membership.**

The blues note is pure tension: passing treatment only. The next lesson adds
two notes at once — one of each kind — and the difference in how they behave
is the whole lesson.

## Practice

1. Loop a slow **Am** vamp. Play familiar pentatonic phrases, and in each one
   place the D# exactly once, always moving through it to E or down to D.
2. Now break the rule on purpose: land on the D# and hold it. Listen to what
   the tension feels like unresolved. Knowing the wrong version trains the ear
   faster than only playing the right one.
3. **Tritone Passing Tone** in [4] Practice is this lesson as a lick — loop
   its backing and steal its treatment for your own phrases.

## What's Next

Two notes sit between the pentatonic scale and the full natural minor scale:
B and F. One of them is safe everywhere. The other is the spiciest note in the
key. Adding them is easy — telling them apart is the skill.
