## Two Sixths, One Decision

The last lesson added F, the b6, with its downward pull. But some minor-key
songs use a different sixth: **F#**, the natural 6. Swap F for F# and the
seven notes A B C D E F# G spell **A Dorian** — the sound of Santana vamps,
funk grooves, and most minor-key jam sessions.

So which one do you play? Here is the part that makes this lesson matter:
it is not a stylistic preference. **The backing chords have already decided,**
and you can read the answer off the chord chart.

## The Deciding Chord

Build a chord on the 4th degree of the key of A minor and the sixth you chose
lands inside it — D–F–A or D–F#–A:

| The 4-chord is… | It contains | So the key's sixth is | Sound |
|-----------------|-------------|----------------------|-------|
| **Dm** (minor iv) | F natural | F — natural minor (Aeolian) | dark, classical, heavy |
| **D** (major IV) | F# | F# — Dorian | warm, jazzy, funky |

Play F natural over a D major chord and you are a half step below the chord's
own F# — a direct clash, and not the interesting kind. Play F# over Dm and the
same collision happens in reverse. The progression votes; the soloist counts
the ballots.

**Am – Dm** vamp → F. **Am – D** vamp → F#. That single chord quality is the
entire Aeolian-versus-Dorian decision, and it is question one to ask of any
minor-key song you solo over.

Compare this with the natural minor box from the last lesson. On the B string
the difference is one fret: **F at fret 6 became F# at fret 7.** (The F# also
appears at fret 4 on the D string, pulling the shape slightly wider.) One fret
of geography, an entirely different climate.

And the new note behaves differently too: where F demanded resolution, **F#
over a Dorian vamp is a color** — you can land on it and let it ring. Over
the D chord it is the chord's own 3rd, the sweetest note available. In the
Key View ([3] Tools), select A Dorian and the 6 carries the ◆ — it is the
mode's characteristic note.

## This Is What Modes Are

If you worked through the Modes track, you may recognize what just happened:
Aeolian versus Dorian, taught not as two patterns to memorize but as one
decision forced by one chord. This is how modes show up in real playing — not
"which mode am I in?" but "which version of the variable note do these chords
want?" The Modes track gives you the full system; this lesson is the system
applied at the moment of soloing.

## Practice

1. Loop **Am – D**, two bars each (the Dorian vamp — Key View has it). Solo in
   the pentatonic box and add *only* F#, B string fret 7. Land on it over the
   D chord and hold — the warmth is the Dorian sound.
2. Re-record the loop as **Am – Dm** and play the same phrases. Your F# now
   sours over Dm. Correct it to F, fret 6, resolving to E. Alternate the two
   loops until your hands pick the sixth from the chord automatically.
3. **The Sixth Swap** in [4] Practice makes the comparison a single lick.
