## What Is a Mode?

A mode is a scale built by starting the major scale on a different degree. The C major scale (C D E F G A B) contains seven notes — and each one can become the root of its own mode, producing seven distinct scales that all use the same pitches but create a different tonal center.

| Degree | Mode | Notes (from C major) | Character |
|--------|------|-----------------------|-----------|
| 1 | Ionian (major) | C D E F G A B | Bright, settled |
| 2 | Dorian | D E F G A B C | Minor with a lifted 6th |
| 3 | Phrygian | E F G A B C D | Dark, Spanish, half-step above root |
| 4 | Lydian | F G A B C D E | Dreamy, floating, raised 4th |
| 5 | Mixolydian | G A B C D E F | Major with flat 7th, bluesy |
| 6 | Aeolian (natural minor) | A B C D E F G | Sad, settled minor |
| 7 | Locrian | B C D E F G A | Unstable, rarely used |

You already know two of these: Ionian is the major scale, and Aeolian is the natural minor scale. Modes are not new scales — they are new perspectives on the scale you already know.

## Two Ways of Thinking About Modes

**Relative thinking** — same notes, different root. D Dorian uses exactly the same notes as C major. If you already know a C major position on the neck, you can play D Dorian from that same box by treating D as home instead of C.

**Parallel thinking** — same root, different intervals. Compare D Dorian directly to D major. D Dorian is D major with a b3 and b7. This tells you what the mode *sounds like* compared to the major scale — which is more useful for choosing modes in practice.

The relative approach helps you find modes quickly on the neck. The parallel approach helps you understand what they sound like and when to use them. You need both.

## The Four Characteristic Modes

Locrian is almost never used in practice — its diminished 5th makes it too unstable to establish a tonal center. The other six are all useful, but four are heard constantly in rock, blues, jazz, and pop:

- **Dorian** — the most common minor mode; has a brighter, more hopeful quality than natural minor
- **Phrygian** — dark and exotic; heavily used in metal and flamenco
- **Lydian** — bright and ethereal; film scores and dreamy pop progressions
- **Mixolydian** — major with a blues twist; the sound of classic rock and funk

The next four lessons cover each of these in depth — one position on the neck, the characteristic interval that defines it, and the progressions that call for it.

## How Modes Sit on the Neck

The five CAGED positions you learned for major and natural minor are the same boxes used for modes — just with a different note treated as the root. This means you do not need to learn any new shapes. You need to learn to *hear and feel* a different center within shapes you already know.

A practical starting point: find the root note of the mode you want to play and locate it within a familiar major-scale box. That box is now your modal position for that key.
