## Mixolydian vs Major

Mixolydian is built on the 5th degree of the major scale. It is the *dominant* mode — the scale that lives on the V chord of a major key. Compared to major (Ionian), it has one change: a **flat 7th** (b7) instead of a natural 7th.

| Scale | Intervals | Notes (key of G) |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| G Major | 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 | G A B C D E F# |
| G Mixolydian | 1 2 3 4 5 6 **b7** | G A B C D E **F** |

That single lowered note — F natural instead of F# — removes the leading tone. The natural 7th (F#) in G major pulls strongly upward to the root; the b7 (F natural) has no such pull. This is what gives Mixolydian its relaxed, settled quality despite sounding major.

The b7 (F natural) appears at fret 8 on string 5 and fret 6 on string 2. These are the notes that distinguish this box from straight G major. When you play F natural (not F#) over a G chord, you are in Mixolydian territory.

## Why Mixolydian Sounds Like Rock

The dominant 7th chord (G7 = G B D F) is built on the Mixolydian scale. The G major pentatonic you already know is contained within G Mixolydian — Mixolydian adds the 4th (C) and the b7 (F). Those two additions, particularly the b7, are what transform the pentatonic sound into something bluesier and more complex.

Classic rock guitar often sits in Mixolydian over the I chord of a major key: the V chord uses Mixolydian *and* the I chord is sometimes treated as Mixolydian when the song stays on it for a long time without resolving to the IV.

Mixolydian is heard in:
- *Norwegian Wood* (Beatles) — Mixolydian flavour on the verse
- *Sweet Home Chicago* (blues standard) — I7 chord implies Mixolydian throughout
- *Hey Joe* (Hendrix) — Mixolydian/blues mixture
- *Takin' Care of Business* (BTO), *La Grange* (ZZ Top) — driving rock riffs

## Mixolydian Progressions

The chord built on the 7th degree of Mixolydian is a major chord (bVII). This **I – bVII** movement is the harmonic fingerprint of Mixolydian in rock:

- **I – bVII** — the core Mixolydian sound (G – F in G Mixolydian)
- **I – bVII – IV** — extended rock loop (G – F – C)
- **I7** — a single dominant 7th chord sustained for a long time implies Mixolydian

If you see a major chord immediately followed by the major chord one whole step *below* it (I – bVII), Mixolydian is the natural scale choice.

## Mixolydian vs Major Pentatonic

You already know the G major pentatonic (G A B D E — degrees 1 2 3 5 6). G Mixolydian adds two notes: the 4th (C) and the b7 (F). The b7 is the important addition — it converts the clean pentatonic sound into the bluesier dominant sound. When you want more colour than pentatonic but a brighter feel than the minor pentatonic, Mixolydian fills that role perfectly.

## Practice

1. Run the scale from the root on string 4 (fret 5) upward through strings 3, 2, 1, then back down through strings 5 and 6.
2. Locate both b7 notes (s5 f8 and s2 f6). These are your Mixolydian colour notes — make sure they appear in your phrases.
3. Improvise over a G7 chord and compare the sound when you use G major pentatonic vs G Mixolydian. Notice how the b7 changes the flavour.
