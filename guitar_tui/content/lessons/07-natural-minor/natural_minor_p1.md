## Position 1 — E-Shape (frets 5–8)

Position 1 has its root on string 6 at fret 5. It is the E-shape position — the same region of the neck as the open E minor chord moved up to A. Three notes appear on most strings, which is the defining fingering characteristic of the full seven-note scale versus the two-notes-per-string pentatonic.

## String-by-String Breakdown

| String | Frets | Degrees |
|--------|-------|---------|
| 6 (low E) | 5, 7, 8 | 1, 2, b3 |
| 5 (A) | 5, 7 | 4, 5 |
| 4 (D) | 5, 7 | b7, 1 |
| 3 (G) | 5, 7 | b3, 4 |
| 2 (B) | 5, 6, 8 | 5, b6, b7 |
| 1 (high e) | 5, 7, 8 | 1, 2, b3 |

The B string is where the most care is required — three notes at frets 5, 6, and 8 create a stretch from fret 5 to 8 with a note sitting between at fret 6. Use the index finger for fret 5, middle for fret 6, and pinky for fret 8.

## Fingering

Standard one-finger-per-fret: index covers fret 5, middle fret 6, ring fret 7, pinky fret 8. Strings 5 and 4 use only two notes (two frets apart) — no stretch required on those strings.

## The b6 on the B String

Fret 6 on the B string (the b6, degree F in key A) is the "extra" note that is not in the minor pentatonic. Approach it by playing frets 5–6–8 on the B string descending — the half-step from 6 to 5 (F to E) is the characteristic sound of the natural minor b6 resolving down to the 5th.

## Practice

1. Ascending and descending through all 15 notes in strict alternate picking.
2. Play three-note groupings: start on string 6, play frets 5–7–8, move to string 5 frets 5–7, etc.
3. Focus phrases on the root notes at fret 5 (strings 6, 4, 1) as arrival points.
