## From Pentatonic to Full Minor

The A minor pentatonic uses five notes: A C D E G (intervals 1 b3 4 5 b7). The *natural minor* scale — also called the Aeolian mode — adds two more: B (the 2nd) and F (the b6). The result is a seven-note scale with a richer, more complex sound that can express darker moods and more varied melodic lines.

| Scale | Intervals | Notes (key of A) |
|-------|-----------|-----------------|
| Minor pentatonic | 1 b3 4 5 b7 | A C D E G |
| Natural minor | 1 2 b3 4 5 b6 b7 | A B C D E F G |

The two added notes — B (2nd) and F (b6) — are what distinguish the natural minor from the pentatonic. The b6 (F in the key of A) is the most characteristic interval: it creates the sad, aching quality heard in classical minor music and many rock ballads.

The pentatonic skeleton is still there — the five pentatonic notes appear at the same frets they always did. The 2nd (B) and b6 (F) slot in around them, filling gaps and creating smoother stepwise movement.

## The b6 — Defining the Sound

The b6 (F in A minor) is the note that most strongly marks this scale as "natural minor" rather than just an extended pentatonic. It creates a half-step above the 5th (E→F), which is a particularly tense interval. In melody, resolving from F down to E — or bending toward F and releasing — is one of the most expressive gestures in minor key playing.

## Comparing Pentatonic and Natural Minor

Playing through Position 1 of both scales back to back makes the relationship clear: the natural minor sounds smoother and more "flowing" because it fills in the larger gaps of the pentatonic. The pentatonic sounds more blues-rough because of those gaps. Both are valid tools — switching between them within a solo is a technique used by rock and classical guitarists alike.

## Practice Approach

1. Learn the shape by playing from the lowest note (string 6, fret 5) to the highest (string 1, fret 8) and back. Three notes per string on most strings.
2. Compare it to the minor pentatonic in the same region — identify which notes are the same and which are the two additions (B on string 6/1, F on string 2).
3. Play the scale descending and focus on landing on root notes — A at fret 5 on strings 6, 4, and 1.

**Natural Minor Descent** in [4] Practice is a phrase that moves stepwise down through the upper strings of Position 1, using the 2nd and b6 — the two notes the pentatonic does not have — as connecting steps. It is a direct demonstration of why the full scale produces smoother lines than the pentatonic.
