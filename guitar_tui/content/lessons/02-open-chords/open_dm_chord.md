## Finger Placement

D minor occupies the same four-string zone as D major, but the fingering rearranges to lower the high e string by one fret. That single change — from fret 2 to fret 1 — is what converts the major sound to minor.

```diagram

```

- **Index finger** — 1st fret, high e string
- **Middle finger** — 2nd fret, G string
- **Ring finger** — 3rd fret, B string

Unlike D major, where the fingers form a symmetric triangle, Dm has the index finger reaching down to the 1st fret while the other two fingers are above. Keep the index finger arched so its tip does not rest against the open D string or B string.

## D Major vs D Minor

The difference is entirely in the high e string:

| String | D major | D minor |
|--------|---------|---------|
| D | 0 (open) | 0 (open) |
| G | 2 (index) | 2 (middle) |
| B | 3 (ring) | 3 (ring) |
| high e | 2 (middle) | 1 (index) |

Strum D major, then move the index to the high e string at fret 1. The shift in mood is immediate and noticeable — this is one of the clearest demonstrations of how a single note change can alter a chord's character entirely.

## Common Mistakes

- **Index finger muting the open D** — the index finger at fret 1 can droop down and touch the open D string below. Keep the finger tipped upright.
- **B string buzz** — the ring finger at fret 3 must press firmly. B string buzz is almost always a pressure issue on the ring finger.
- **Swapping Dm and D fingering** — because the shapes are so similar, it is easy to play one when you intend the other. Slow down and confirm finger placement before strumming.
