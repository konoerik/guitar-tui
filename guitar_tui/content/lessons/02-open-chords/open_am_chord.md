## Finger Placement

A minor is structurally similar to A major — five strings, low E muted, two fretted notes on the 2nd fret — but the finger arrangement shifts, and the result is a noticeably darker, more melancholic sound.

```diagram

title: A Minor
frets: [null, 0, 2, 2, 1, 0]
fingers: [null, null, 2, 3, 1, null]
```

- **Index finger** — 1st fret, B string
- **Middle finger** — 2nd fret, D string
- **Ring finger** — 2nd fret, G string

The index finger sits alone at the 1st fret while the middle and ring fingers are one fret higher. The critical challenge is keeping the index finger arched enough to leave the open high e string completely free.

The B string at fret 1 requires the index finger to press very close to the 1st fret wire. Buzzing on B means the finger is too far back.

## A Major vs A Minor

Compare the two shapes side by side:

| String | A major | A minor |
|--------|---------|---------|
| D | 2 (index) | 2 (middle) |
| G | 2 (middle) | 2 (ring) |
| B | 2 (ring) | 1 (index) |
| high e | 0 (open) | 0 (open) |

The single-fret shift of the B string is what changes major to minor. This is a useful thing to hear: strum A major, then lift the ring finger and place the index at B/fret 1, and listen to the mood shift with one adjustment.

## Common Mistakes

- **High e muted** — the index finger at fret 1 must arch steeply so its tip does not rest against the high e string. This is the most common problem with Am.
- **B string buzz** — index finger too far from the fret wire. Move the tip closer.
- **Low E accidentally strummed** — start the strum from the A string. With practice this becomes automatic.
