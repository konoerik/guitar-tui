## What Is a Minor 7th Chord?

A minor 7th chord (written Xm7) takes a minor chord and adds the *minor* 7th — the note one whole step below the octave. Formula: **1 – b3 – 5 – b7**.

Minor 7th chords are cool and understated. The minor 3rd gives the darkness of a minor chord, and the minor 7th softens it further — removing some of the starkness of a plain minor chord. They are everywhere in jazz, funk, and R&B. Diatonically, they appear on the ii, iii, and vi degrees of major keys, and on the i degree of minor keys.

## The Shape Relationship

Compare each minor 7th to its plain minor version:

- **Em7 vs Em**: Em frets the A string at fret 2 (B = 5th) and the D string at fret 2 (E = root). Em7 keeps only A string fret 2; all other strings ring open. The D (open D string) is the b7 of E.
- **Am7 vs Am**: Am frets the G string at fret 2 (C# — wait, no: A minor has C natural, so G string fret 2 = A = root). Am7 opens the G string (G = b7 of A). One finger lifted.
- **Dm7 vs Dm**: Dm has the B string at fret 3 (D = root). Dm7 moves it to fret 1 (C = b7 of D) and mini-barres the top two strings. The high e at fret 1 is also C.
- **Bm7 vs Bm**: Standard Bm is a barre chord (fret 2). Bm7 is a striking open-string shape: A string fret 2 (B = root), open D (D = b3), G string fret 2 (A = b7), open B (B = root), high e fret 2 (F# = 5th). It avoids the barre entirely.

## Diatonic Role

In a major key, three of the seven diatonic chords are minor 7ths:

| Key | ii m7 | iii m7 | vi m7 |
|-----|-------|--------|-------|
| G major | Am7 | Bm7 | Em7 |
| D major | Em7 | F#m7 | Bm7 |
| A major | Bm7 | C#m7 | F#m7 |

The ii m7 → V7 → Imaj7 progression (e.g., Am7 → D7 → Gmaj7 in G major) is the most important harmonic motion in jazz. The ii m7 substitutes for IV, creates smooth voice-leading into the V7, and the V7 resolves to Imaj7.

## Minor 7th as Colour

Plain minor chords can feel stark or sad. Minor 7th chords soften that edge — they are melancholy without being harsh, smooth rather than heavy. Swapping Am for Am7, or Em for Em7, in a pop or folk progression instantly adds a jazz-influenced warmth.
