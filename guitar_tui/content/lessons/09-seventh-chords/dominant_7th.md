## What Is a Dominant 7th Chord?

A dominant 7th chord (written X7) takes a major chord and adds a *minor* 7th — the note that is one whole step below the octave. Formula: **1 – 3 – 5 – b7**.

The name "dominant" comes from the fact that the V chord of a major key naturally has this formula. In C major, G–B–D–F is the diatonic 7th chord on the 5th degree — G dominant 7th (G7). The b7 (F) creates intense tension that resolves down a half-step to the 3rd of the I chord (E in C major), making V7→I one of the most powerful resolutions in music.

## The Shape Relationship

Compare each 7th chord to its major version:
- **E7 vs E**: E has G# on the G string (fret 1). E7 removes it, letting the G string ring open (G natural = the b7 of E).
- **A7 vs A**: A has fret 2 on the G string (A = root). A7 opens the G string (G = b7 of A).
- **G7 vs G**: G adds F on the high e string (fret 1) to a G major shape — that F is the b7.
- **D7 vs D**: D has fret 3 on the B string (D = root). D7 moves it to fret 1 (C = b7 of D).

In each case, adding or moving one note transforms the major chord into a dominant 7th.

## Why 7ths Add Momentum

A straight major chord is stable — it can sit without needing to resolve anywhere. A dominant 7th chord contains a tritone (the interval between the 3rd and b7), which is maximally dissonant and demands resolution. This is why the V7 chord creates such a strong pull toward I.

In blues and rock, dominant 7ths are used on the I chord as well (not just V), because the added tension gives the music a grittier, bluesier character even when the chord is "at home."

## The 12-Bar Blues with Seventh Chords

The standard blues uses dominant 7ths on all three chords (I7, IV7, V7):

| Blues key | I7 | IV7 | V7 |
|-----------|-----|-----|-----|
| A | A7 | D7 | E7 |
| E | E7 | A7 | B7 |
| G | G7 | C7 | D7 |

Replacing the plain major chords with their 7th versions immediately transforms a simple progression into the blues harmonic vocabulary.
