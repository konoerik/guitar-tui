## Beyond Chord Names

So far the progressions in this track have been named by their chord letters — G–D–Em–C, A–D–E. But chord letters alone do not capture the most useful insight: *the same musical relationship can appear in any key*.

Roman numerals are the tool for thinking about this. Instead of saying "G, C, D", you say "I, IV, V in G". The numerals describe the *function* of each chord, and the same function holds in every key. This is how musicians transpose songs, communicate chord relationships, and understand why certain progressions always feel the same regardless of key.

## How the Numbering Works

In any major key, each note of the scale gets a number. The chord built on that note gets the same Roman numeral. In G major:

| Degree | Note | Chord | Numeral |
|--------|------|-------|---------|
| 1st | G | G major | **I** |
| 2nd | A | A minor | ii |
| 3rd | B | B minor | iii |
| 4th | C | C major | **IV** |
| 5th | D | D major | **V** |
| 6th | E | E minor | vi |
| 7th | F# | F# dim | vii° |

**Uppercase** = major chord. **Lowercase** = minor chord.

The I, IV, and V chords are always major in a major key. That pattern holds regardless of which key you are in.

## The Same Relationship in A

Change the key to A and the same Roman numerals map to different chords — but the *sound and function* of the relationship is identical:

| Numeral | In G | In A |
|---------|------|------|
| I | G | A |
| IV | C | D |
| V | D | E |

You already played I–IV–V in A in the previous lesson (A–D–E), even if it was not called that at the time.

## The V Chord — Why It Pulls

The V chord has a specific gravitational pull toward the I chord. This tension and release is the heartbeat of blues, country, and classical music alike. In G major, D wants to resolve to G. In A major, E wants to resolve to A. Play V–I slowly and listen for the release on the I chord — once you hear it, you will notice it in music everywhere.

## Using This Knowledge

Roman numerals let you:

- **Transpose any progression** — if you know a song is I–IV–V, you can play it in any key by finding those three chords for that key.
- **Communicate with other musicians** — "I–IV–V in A" is unambiguous. "A–D–E" requires the other player to know what key you're in.
- **Understand song structure** — most songwriting analysis uses Roman numerals. The more you think in numerals rather than chord names, the more connections you will see across different songs and styles.

## Next Steps

The vi chord (the minor chord on the 6th degree) is the missing piece of most pop progressions. In G major that is Em; in C major it is Am. The I–V–vi–IV progression you will encounter constantly is just I–IV–V with the vi chord inserted: G–D–Em–C in G, or C–G–Am–F in C.

Once Roman numerals feel natural, the theory section of this app will make considerably more sense — every scale pattern and chord relationship is described in these terms.
