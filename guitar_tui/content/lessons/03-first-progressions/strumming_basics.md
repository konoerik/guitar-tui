## Why Rhythm Comes Before More Chords

A guitarist who plays the right chords with sloppy timing sounds worse than one
who plays simple chords in a solid groove. Rhythm is the container that makes
harmony musical. Before learning new chord shapes or scales, it pays to spend
dedicated time on how you strum the chords you already know.

Every progression in this track — G–D–Em–C, C–G–Am–F, the 12-bar blues — can
sound bland or compelling depending on the rhythm underneath it. The patterns
in this lesson are the difference.

## The Two Motions: Down and Up

Every strumming pattern is a combination of two hand movements:

- **Downstroke (D):** Hand sweeps from the lowest string toward the highest
- **Upstroke (U):** Hand sweeps from the highest string toward the lowest

The critical technique point: **the hand moves continuously like a pendulum.**
On beats where you skip a strum (a *ghost strum*), the hand still moves through
the motion — it simply misses the strings. If the hand stops between strokes,
the rhythm becomes mechanical and stiff.

## The Count: 1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and

Standard 4/4 time has four beats per bar. The "and" falls exactly halfway
between each beat — the off-beat. Counting out loud builds the internal clock
that keeps you in time.

```
Count:   1    and    2    and    3    and    4    and
Hand:    D     U     D     U     D     U     D     U
```

Strumming every downstroke and upstroke gives eight strokes per bar. Most
patterns use a selection of these eight possible positions.

## Pattern 1: Four Down Strums

```
Count:   1          2          3          4
Strum:   D          D          D          D
```

The simplest pattern and the right place to start. One downstroke per beat,
four beats per bar. Use this to establish clean chord changes before adding
rhythmic complexity. When you can switch chords on every beat without hesitation,
you are ready for the patterns below.

## Pattern 2: Down-Down-Up (3/4 Time)

Three beats per bar — the *waltz* feel. Common in ballads and folk music.

```
Count:   1          2    and   3
Strum:   D          D     U    D
```

Try this over Am – F – C – G, three beats per chord. It has a rolling, forward
motion that suits minor-key material.

## Pattern 3: The Folk Strum (D DU UDU)

The most widely used beginner pattern in pop and folk. Seven strokes across
four beats, with one ghost strum on the "and" of beat 2:

```
Count:   1     and   2     and        and   3     and   4     and
Strum:   D           D      U               U     D      U
```

Written compactly: **D  DU  UDU**

The gap on the "and" of 2 — where your hand moves up but misses the strings —
is what gives the pattern its characteristic lilt and forward motion. Practice
it slowly with G – D – Em – C before increasing tempo. This pattern underlies
an enormous number of pop songs.

## Pattern 4: Straight Eighths (D U D U D U D U)

All eight positions — every downstroke and every upstroke, no gaps:

```
Count:   1     and   2     and   3     and   4     and
Strum:   D      U    D      U    D      U    D      U
```

This is not a song pattern — it is a coordination drill. Play it at 60 BPM
with a single chord and listen for evenness: every stroke should sound the same
volume, and the spacing should be perfectly regular. Most beginners find
upstrokes quieter than downstrokes. Record yourself and listen back.

Once even at 60 BPM, use it as the foundation for any song pattern: you are
selecting *which* of these eight positions to actually strum. Pattern 3 (the
folk strum) is just Pattern 4 with three positions ghosted.

## Applying Patterns to the Progressions You Know

Work through this sequence with G–D–Em–C:

1. **Four down strums** — one chord per bar, clean transitions first
2. **D DU UDU** at 60 BPM — two bars per chord, then one bar per chord
3. **Full 4/4** at 60 BPM — only increase tempo once the pattern feels effortless

Then repeat the exercise with C–G–Am–F and the A–D–E progression. The goal is
to decouple the strumming hand from your conscious attention so your focus is
free for chord changes and dynamics.

## Common Mistakes

**Stopping the hand between strokes.** The pendulum motion must be continuous.
If you find yourself pausing, slow down until the motion becomes natural at that
tempo.

**Rushing when strumming feels hard.** Use a metronome or drum track. Start at
60 BPM and only move up when the pattern requires no mental effort. Tempo built
slowly is durable; tempo rushed is fragile.

**Strumming all six strings on every chord.** Some shapes have muted strings
(Bm, F, Am). Aim the strum to cover only the strings that should ring — this
is easier with a lighter touch and a slight wrist rotation on upstrokes.

**Losing your place in the bar.** Count out loud ("1 and 2 and 3 and 4 and")
until the count becomes an internal pulse you no longer need to voice.
