## Why This Progression

A, D, and E are the I, IV, and V chords of the A major scale. The I–IV–V relationship is arguably the most important in Western music: blues, country, early rock and roll, folk, and reggae all rely on it. Knowing it in A gives you a working framework to understand any key.

What makes this progression feel different from C–G–Am–F is the *energy*. There is no minor chord softening the mood — A, D, and E are all major, giving the progression a bright, driving quality that suits an upbeat or bluesy feel.

## The I–IV–V Relationship

In the key of A:

| Numeral | Chord | Notes |
|---------|-------|-------|
| I | A major | A – C# – E |
| IV | D major | D – F# – A |
| V | E major | E – G# – B |

The IV chord (D) and V chord (E) share notes with the I chord (A). That shared note content is part of why the I–IV–V movement feels so natural and resolved. The V chord (E) has the strongest pull back to the I chord (A) — this is the tension-and-release mechanism at the heart of blues phrasing.

## Transitions

1. **A → D** — moderate. All three fingers lift and regroup. The move from three adjacent frets (A) to a triangular cluster (D) requires deliberate practice.
2. **D → E** — easy. The D shape shifts down one string and expands slightly. Many players find this the smoothest change in the set.
3. **E → A** — moderate. The hand fully resets. Practice lifting from E while mentally preparing the A fingering before your pick reaches the strings.

## Common Problems

- **D string buzz on A major** — the three-finger cluster at fret 2 needs each fingertip perpendicular to the strings. Any tilt allows a finger to rest on the D string.
- **B string muted on E major** — the index finger at fret 1 of the G string is the culprit. Raise the knuckle.
- **Rushing the V–I resolution** — when E resolves to A, there is a natural musical tendency to move quickly. Keep the tempo steady; the resolution will feel satisfying without rushing.
