## Why This Progression

C, G, Am, and F are the four diatonic chords of the *C major scale* that appear most often together. They work in any order and can be rearranged to produce very different emotional results. You will hear this progression — or a rotation of it — in countless songs across pop, rock, folk, and country.

The rotation starting on Am (Am–F–C–G) gives a more melancholic feel. Starting on F (F–C–G–Am) sounds anthemic. All four starting points use identical chord shapes — only the emotional emphasis changes.

All six strings ring. The ring finger on the high e must arch sharply to avoid muting the B string.

Five strings. Mute the low E. The index finger at fret 1 on the B string must not lean into the open high e.

## The Transitions

The critical changes in this progression:

1. **C → G** — a full hand reset. Every finger relocates. Practise this pair most.
2. **G → Am** — moderate. The ring finger anchors on the D string; the other fingers converge onto the Am shape.
3. **Am → F** — the hardest transition if F is still unfamiliar. The index finger must lay flat as all fingers shift.
4. **F → C** — satisfying once the barre is comfortable. The ring finger lifts from A/D to land on A alone.

Use one-minute changes: set a timer for 60 seconds and alternate just two chords, counting only the clean transitions.

## Common Problems

- **F chord buzz** — if the barre is the limiting factor, practise it daily in isolation. The barre at fret 1 is the hardest position on the neck; fret 3 or 5 (same shape, lower tension) is a good place to build strength before returning to fret 1.
- **C → G transition stalling** — try lifting all fingers simultaneously and landing them in the G shape as a single unit, rather than placing fingers one by one.
- **Am → F losing time** — slow the tempo until both chords sound clean, then gradually increase. Speed is a consequence of clean repetition.

## Next Steps

Once the loop is clean, try starting on Am instead of C: Am–F–C–G. It has a noticeably more serious, minor feel — yet uses exactly the same four chords. The G–D–Em–C progression you learned earlier is in the same key (G major); experimenting with both sets of chords together gives you a broad vocabulary in a single key.
