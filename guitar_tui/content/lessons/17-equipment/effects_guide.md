## What an Effect Is

An effect takes your guitar signal and changes it — adds space, repeats it, thickens it, clips it. Effects come as individual **pedals** (stompboxes wired between guitar and amp), as multi-effects units, or built into modeling amps and apps. The vocabulary below is the same everywhere.

## The Glossary

**Overdrive / Distortion / Fuzz** — three intensities of the same idea: clipping the signal. *Overdrive* is warm breakup, like a tube amp pushed hard — blues and classic rock. *Distortion* is harder, more compressed — hard rock and metal rhythm. *Fuzz* is the extreme: splattery, woolly, vintage — think 60s psychedelia. All three add sustain and compress your dynamics.

**Reverb** — the sound of a space: thousands of tiny reflections, as in a room, a hall, or a spring tank inside an amp. A little makes everything sound finished; a lot makes everything sound distant. Most amps have some built in.

**Delay (echo)** — distinct repeats of what you play, at a set time interval and feedback (number of repeats). "Echo" is the older name for the same effect. Short single repeats ("slapback") are rockabilly and country; longer rhythmic repeats are U2 and ambient playing. Delay time can often be tapped in to match the song's tempo.

**Chorus** — a slightly detuned, slightly delayed copy of your signal blended with the original, as if two guitars played the same part. Shimmery and wide — the default clean sound of the 80s.

**Phaser / Flanger** — cousins of chorus: a sweeping, swirling filter motion. Phaser is a soft whoosh; flanger is a more metallic jet-plane sweep.

**Compressor** — narrows the gap between your loudest and quietest notes: quiet notes come up, spiky attacks come down, sustain appears longer. Country chicken-pickers and funk players love it. Note what it trades away: *your* dynamics.

**Wah** — a foot-rocked filter sweep, from muffled to piercing. Voice-like; funk rhythm and expressive lead.

**Tuner pedal** — not an effect, but it lives on every pedalboard, always first in the chain, and mutes the signal while tuning. The most important pedal on this list.

**Chain order** (convention, not law): tuner → compressor → drive → modulation (chorus/phaser) → delay → reverb. Time-based effects go last so they echo the finished sound rather than getting distorted.

## Effects and Practice: a Warning

Two of the most beautiful effects are also the two that lie to you:

- **Delay and reverb blur timing.** The repeats and wash fill the gaps between your notes — including the gaps that are timing errors. The rhythm work in Track 14 and the phrasing work in Track 13 (where rests are load-bearing) should be practiced **dry or nearly dry**. Add the ambience back when the timing is real.
- **Distortion and compression flatten dynamics.** Your accents, ghost strokes, and pick control are inaudible under heavy gain. Practice the mechanics clean; perform with whatever you like.

The honest workflow: practice dry, record dry, listen — then decorate.

## Do You Need Any of This?

No. Everything this app teaches works with a bare guitar. A reasonable starter set for the sounds in these lessons: the amp's own reverb, one overdrive, and — genuinely more useful than any of them — the looper in the next lesson. Buy effects to chase a specific sound you keep hearing, not as a substitute for one.

## Common Mistakes

- **Stacking everything at once** — five effects deep, no idea which knob does what. Add one, learn it, then add the next.
- **Using reverb as a blanket** — if every note swims, the reverb is hiding your articulation from you.
- **Chasing a player's pedals instead of their hands** — the tone you admire is mostly fingers, timing, and note choice. The pedalboard is the last 10%.
