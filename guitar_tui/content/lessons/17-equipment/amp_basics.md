## Half the Instrument

An electric guitar's sound is made twice: once by the strings, once by the amplifier. Two players with the same guitar and different amp settings sound like different instruments. Understanding five knobs gets you most of the way.

The signal path is simple: guitar → (any pedals) → amp input → **preamp** (where gain and EQ live) → **power amp** (where loudness lives) → speaker.

## Gain vs. Volume — the Big One

These two knobs confuse everyone at first because both make things louder at low settings:

- **Gain** (also labeled *drive*) sets how hard your signal hits the preamp. Low gain: clean, glassy, dynamic. Higher gain: the preamp starts to clip the signal — first warm breakup, then crunch, then full distortion. Gain is a **tone** control disguised as a loudness control.
- **Volume** (or *master*) sets how loud the final result is. It does not change the character, just the amount.

The combination is the point: **high gain + low master** gives you saturated rock tone at bedroom volume. Low gain + higher master gives you loud, clean punch. If your amp has one volume knob and no gain, it does both jobs at once — breakup arrives only at volume.

## EQ: Bass, Mid, Treble

- **Bass** — thickness and weight. Too much and chords turn muddy, especially with distortion.
- **Middle** — the guitar's actual voice. The guitar *is* a midrange instrument; mids are what let it cut through a band or a backing track. "Scooping" the mids (bass and treble up, mids near zero) sounds huge alone and vanishes completely in a mix.
- **Treble** — definition and sparkle. Too much turns picking noise into ice picks.

Start everything at 12 o'clock, play, and move one knob at a time. There is no correct setting — there is only what suits the song and the room.

Some amps add **presence** (upper-treble edge, after the EQ) and **reverb** (built-in echo of a space — covered properly in the next lesson).

## Tube, Solid-State, Modeling

Three technologies, one honest summary: **tube** amps clip gradually and respond to picking dynamics in a way players love, at the cost of weight, price, and volume needed to shine. **Solid-state** amps are consistent, affordable, and reliable. **Modeling** amps digitally imitate many amps and effects in one box — the most practice-friendly option, usually with headphone output. All three are legitimate; nobody in a blind test cares as much as the internet does.

## Settings for Practice

Practice tone and performance tone are different jobs:

1. **Practice with less gain than you enjoy.** Distortion compresses and sustains — it *hides* uneven picking, sloppy muting, and buzzing frets. Clean settings report your actual playing. Earn the gain.
2. **Mids up.** You will hear your note definition, and chords will separate instead of smearing.
3. **Match the backing track's level.** If the amp drowns the looper or metronome, you will drift in time without noticing (Track 14's subdivision work depends on hearing the grid).
4. **Headphone out is not cheating.** Late-night practice that happens beats loud practice that doesn't.

## Common Mistakes

- **Buying tone instead of practicing** — a new amp will not fix timing. The players you admire sound good through anything.
- **Setting EQ by eye** — 12 o'clock everywhere is a starting point, not a rule; rooms and guitars differ. Use your ears, one knob at a time.
- **Practicing at whisper volume forever** — dynamics are a skill; sometimes you need enough level to practice controlling loud and soft.
