## The Assumed Tool

Every lick in [4] Practice ends with a **Looper Setup** section: chords, tempo, bar count. The phrasing and rhythm tracks send you to "the looper workflow" again and again. This lesson pays that debt: what the tool is and how to drive it.

A **looper** records a phrase you play and repeats it back endlessly. You strum four bars of Am, and the looper becomes a tireless rhythm guitarist playing those four bars forever while you practice scales, licks, and phrasing over the top. It is the closest thing to a band that fits on the floor — and for self-directed practice it beats backing tracks, because *you* choose the chords, tempo, and length, exactly as each lick's setup specifies.

## Forms It Comes In

- **Looper pedal** — a stompbox (many cost less than a video game). Hands stay on the guitar; one button does the work. The reference for this lesson.
- **Phone or tablet apps** — free-to-cheap, fine for getting started; less convenient to punch in and out while holding a guitar.
- **Amp or multi-effects built-ins** — many modeling amps include one; check yours before buying anything.

## Driving a Single-Button Looper

The near-universal pattern (check your pedal's manual — details vary):

1. **Press** — recording starts *the instant you press*.
2. **Press again** — the loop closes and playback begins immediately.
3. **Press again** — overdub: play on top, layering onto the loop.
4. **Double-tap** — stop. **Press and hold** — clear the loop.

The skill is in presses 1 and 2: they define the seam.

## Recording a Clean Loop

A loop that stutters or gaps at the seam is useless for timing practice — and the seam is pure timing:

1. **Count in with the metronome** — [3] Tools has one. Set it to the lick's BPM and let it run at least a full bar before you start.
2. **Press record exactly on beat 1** as you play the first strum. Treat the press as a note: it has a slot in the count.
3. **Play the full pattern** for the stated number of bars, keeping the strumming pattern constant — a steady pattern hides the seam; a flourish in the last bar exposes it.
4. **Close the loop on the "1" of the bar *after* your last bar** — the same instant beat 1 would come around again. You are pressing *on* the downbeat, not after it.
5. **Listen to two full cycles before soloing.** If the seam hiccups, clear it and re-record — thirty seconds now saves ten minutes of practicing against a limping beat.

Keep loops short: two or four bars. Long loops mean long waits for the section you want to practice, and more chances to drift while recording.

## Using It with This App

The workflow every lick expects:

1. Open the lick in [4] Practice; read its **Looper Setup** (chords, tempo, bars).
2. Set the metronome to the stated BPM; record the loop as above.
3. Practice the lick over the loop — slowly at first, watching the *landing* notes: most setups tell you which note should arrive on which chord.
4. When it sits well, stop reading and improvise: the loop doesn't care how many times you try an idea.

The two-bar-rule and rhythm-echo exercises, the phrasing track's question-and-answer work, and the coming ear training track are all built on this exact loop-then-play cycle.

## Common Mistakes

- **Recording without a count-in** — a loop recorded freehand has no grid; everything you practice over it inherits the wobble.
- **Pressing late on the seam** (the most common fault) — press *on* the downbeat as you strike it. If your loop always ends with a gap, you are waiting for the bar to finish before pressing; the press *is* the start of the next cycle.
- **Loops that are too interesting** — a backing loop is a canvas: simple strums, steady pattern. Save the fancy playing for what goes on top.
- **Practicing over a bad loop** — you would never practice with a broken metronome; a lumpy loop is the same thing wearing a disguise.
