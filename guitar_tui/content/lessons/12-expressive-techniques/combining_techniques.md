## Techniques Are Vocabulary

Each technique carries a specific emotional quality:

| Technique | Sound quality |
|-----------|---------------|
| Hammer-on | Forward motion, building momentum |
| Pull-off | Inward, falling motion |
| Slide | Connection, smoothness between ideas |
| Bend | Reaching, tension at the phrase peak |
| Vibrato | Sustain, weight, the note singing |

Skilled phrasing uses these the way a singer shapes a melody — not at random, but in service of the musical line. Tension builds toward a peak; the peak resolves to rest.

## Slide Into a Bend

Sliding into a bend is a classic blues move. The slide provides the approach; the bend provides the color:

The slide from fret 5 (E) to fret 8 (G) arrives with momentum, then the bend pushes that G up to A (the root). Three gestures — pick, slide, bend — but the listener hears one unbroken phrase.

## Building Phrase Shape

Think of a phrase as having three sections:

1. **Approach** — hammer-ons and pull-offs create forward motion without interruption
2. **Peak** — a bend, usually the highest or most dissonant note in the phrase
3. **Resolution** — release the bend back, slide down, or step to a root note

Not every phrase needs all three sections. A single bent note over silence can be enough. The key is knowing *why* you are using each technique, not just that you can.

## Practice Strategy

Do not practice techniques in isolation indefinitely. Once each individual technique is clean, combine them:

1. Choose one lick from [4] Practice that uses multiple techniques
2. Play it at half speed until every technique sounds as intended
3. Increase tempo only when the phrase sounds *musical*, not just mechanically correct
4. Record yourself — your ear detects unevenness more reliably when listening back
