## The High-Neck Shape

Position 4 sits in the upper register of the guitar, centered around frets 12–15. It is the highest of the five positions before the pattern wraps back around. Playing in this register produces a bright, piercing tone and requires the picking hand to work more precisely — the strings are closer together in terms of space, and the lighter touch of the upper neck means small technique variations become more audible.

The low E and A strings both span three frets (fret 12 to 15) — a wider stretch than the two-fret spans used on D, G, and B. The B string is also distinctive: its two notes sit at frets 13 and 15, offset by one fret from the pattern on the adjacent strings.

## Root Notes

The roots sit on the A string (fret 12) and G string (fret 14). The A string root at fret 12 is the same landmark as in Position 3 — the double-dot fret, A one octave above open. The G string root at fret 14 is new territory; it sits between the two double-dot inlays on most guitars (12th and 15th/17th).

## Fingering

Position 4 does not fit the one-finger-per-fret rule neatly because of the three-fret spans. A practical approach:

- **Low E and A strings**: index at fret 12, pinky at fret 15 (stretch of three frets)
- **D and G strings**: index at fret 12, ring at fret 14 (two-fret span)
- **B string**: index at fret 13, pinky at fret 15
- **High e string**: index at fret 12, pinky at fret 15

On the B string, the index shifts up to fret 13 rather than staying at fret 12 — the note at fret 12 on the B string is not in the scale. This shift is easy to miss when running through the shape.

## Practice Approach

1. **Memorise the B string exception** first — it is the most common source of errors in Position 4.
2. **Work the low E and A stretches** as a dedicated exercise: plant the index at fret 12 and reach the pinky to fret 15, alternating between them on each string.
3. **Connect from Position 3** — Position 3 ends at fret 12 across most strings; Position 4 begins there. The 12th fret is the handoff point.

## The Sound of Position 4

Because both roots are in the middle of the shape rather than at the bottom, Position 4 invites phrases that start high and resolve downward to the root. Descending lines from the high e and B strings into the G or A string root have a natural falling quality that suits slower, expressive playing. The b7 degree on the low E string (fret 15) is also a useful starting point for blues phrases — the ear hears it as a dissonant departure that resolves convincingly back to the root.
