## Two Pentatonics, One Relationship

The major pentatonic uses intervals **1 – 2 – 3 – 5 – 6** — it leaves out the 4th and the 7th from the major scale, keeping only the notes that sound universally consonant. The result is bright, open, and melodically clear.

The key insight: **C major pentatonic and A minor pentatonic share the exact same notes.** C–D–E–G–A versus A–C–D–E–G. Same pitches, different home base. They are *relative pentatonics*. This means the five position shapes you already learned for A minor pentatonic are also the five positions for C major pentatonic — just with different root notes highlighted and a different note treated as "home."

## Position 1 — E-Shape (C major, key of C)

Position 1 of the C major pentatonic sits at the 8th fret, with the root C on string 6, fret 8.

Compare this shape to Position 2 of the A minor pentatonic (also frets 7–10). They are identical in physical layout — but when you play the major pentatonic version, you treat fret 8 on the low E string as home, not fret 7 on the D string.

## The Sound Difference

The minor pentatonic has a blues-inflected, dark quality because of its b3 (minor third) — the note that gives it its characteristic tension. The major pentatonic replaces the b3 with a natural 3rd, replacing that tension with brightness and resolution.

Both scales are heavily used in country, pop, rock, and blues. The shift between them over the same chord changes is one of the most expressive tools a guitarist has — playing a minor pentatonic phrase that "resolves" by landing on a major pentatonic note can sound like the sun breaking through clouds.

## Practice Approach

1. **Identify the roots first** — C on string 6 fret 8, string 4 fret 10, string 1 fret 8. These are your landing points for phrases.
2. **Play the scale ascending and descending** from the lowest note (string 5, fret 7) to the highest (string 1, fret 10) and back.
3. **Compare with the A minor shape** — if you have Position 2 of A minor memorised, play it both ways: starting on the D string root (A minor feel) then starting on the low E root (C major feel). Listen to how the same notes produce two entirely different emotional characters.
