## The Octave Landmark

Position 3 is anchored around fret 12 — the octave. Fret 12 on any string produces the same note as the open string, one octave higher. The 12th fret is also visually marked with a double dot inlay on most guitars, making it an unmistakable landmark.

In Position 3, the root note appears on the A string at fret 12 and the B string at fret 10. The A string root is particularly important: fret 12 on the A string is *A*, one octave above the open string. This is a pitch reference you can always find instantly.

Note the stretch on the G string: frets 9 and 12 are three frets apart, which is wider than the usual two-fret span. Keep the index finger at fret 9 and reach the pinky to fret 12. A lower wrist position (toward the floor) will help the stretch feel natural.

Also note the high e side: fret 13 on the B string is one fret above the double-dot marker. This is the only note in this position that sits above fret 12.

## Fingering

- **Fret 9** — index finger (G string only)
- **Fret 10** — index finger (low E, A, D, B, high e)
- **Fret 12** — pinky (all strings except B)
- **Fret 13** — pinky (B string only)

The shape breaks the standard one-finger-per-fret rule on the G string because of the three-fret stretch. Elsewhere, treat fret 10 as index territory and fret 12 as pinky territory.

## Practice Approach

1. **Start on the A string root** (fret 12). This gives you an immediate anchor.
2. **Work the G string stretch** as a focused exercise before running the full shape. Index at fret 9, pinky at fret 12 — alternate between them slowly to build the reach.
3. **Connect from Position 2** — the top of Position 2 ends at fret 10 (high e, degree 4). Position 3 picks up on the same fret range. Play across the boundary: Position 2 notes on high e at frets 8 and 10, then continue down into Position 3.

## The High Register

Position 3 lives in the upper-mid register of the guitar. Soloing here produces a brighter, more cutting tone than Positions 1 or 2. Many classic rock solos in A or E minor use this zone extensively — the 12th fret area of the G, B, and high e strings is particularly resonant and articulate for string bends. Once you are comfortable with the shape, experiment with bending the b3 (fret 10, D string) up a whole step to hear how it affects the sound.
