## Same Notes, Different Stories

The C major pentatonic and A minor pentatonic are *relative pentatonics* — they share the same five notes: C, D, E, G, A. What changes is the root, the "home" note your phrases return to.

- **A minor pentatonic**: treats A as home — sounds blues, rock, dark
- **C major pentatonic**: treats C as home — sounds country, folk, bright

When you are playing over a chord progression in A minor (Am–F–C–G, for example), using the minor pentatonic keeps the dark, blues character. Shifting to resolve phrases on C gives a brighter, more resolved quality. Experienced players switch between the two interpretations fluidly within a single solo.

## Choosing Which to Use

The rule of thumb:

- **Minor chord / minor progression** → minor pentatonic leads to resolution
- **Major chord / major progression** → major pentatonic leads to resolution
- **Over the I–IV–V in a major key** → major pentatonic fits naturally without any "outside" tension
- **12-bar blues** → the minor pentatonic is traditionally favoured even over major chords, because the blues vocabulary embraces that clash — the b3 over a major chord creates the signature blues tension

## The Mixing Trick

The most expressive approach is to use both. Over an A minor chord progression, you can:

1. Start a phrase with A minor pentatonic (dark, bluesy)
2. Resolve it to C (the major 3rd of A minor is the relative major root) using a C major pentatonic phrase (bright landing)

This shifting between minor and relative major pentatonic within a single phrase is what gives blues-rock solos their sense of motion and narrative — tension built with minor, release via major.

## Identifying Which Is Which

A practical test: play a phrase and land on the root note. If landing on A sounds resolved and musical, you are in A minor pentatonic mode. If landing on C sounds resolved, you are in C major pentatonic mode. Both are "correct" over the same five notes — the tonal character comes from *where you land*, not which notes you play.
