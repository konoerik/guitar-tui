## What Makes a Blues Scale?

The blues scale is the minor pentatonic with one added note: the **b5** (also called the *tritone* or *blue note*). In the key of A, that note is D# (Eb). It sits exactly halfway between the root and the fifth — the most dissonant interval possible in Western music — and that dissonance is precisely what gives it its expressive power.

| Minor pentatonic | Blues scale |
|-----------------|-------------|
| 1 – b3 – 4 – 5 – b7 | 1 – b3 – 4 – **b5** – 5 – b7 |

The blue note is not a note you rest on. It is a *passing tone* — it creates tension as you move through it on the way to the 4th or 5th, and that brief moment of tension and release is the blues vocabulary in miniature.

## How to Use the Blue Note

**Use it as a chromatic connector.** On the A string in Position 1, the sequence of adjacent frets is: 5 (4th), 6 (b5), 7 (5th). Sliding up through all three — 4 → b5 → 5 — is one of the most recognizable blues moves on the guitar.

**Bend through it.** Instead of playing the b5 cleanly, bend the 4th up to the b5 and release it back. This smeared approach is closer to how the note is actually heard in classic blues — not as a distinct pitch but as an expressive gesture.

**Avoid resting on it.** The b5 sounds dissonant in isolation. Its power comes from movement — resolving to the 4th below or the 5th above. A phrase that stalls on the b5 will sound unresolved. A phrase that passes through it on the way to a stable note will sound intensely expressive.

Three licks in [4] Practice put this into context. **The B.B. King Box** uses bends and vibrato within the box shape to demonstrate how the blue note sounds in the hands of the style that defined it. **Tritone Pass** moves from A7 to D7 using the b5 as a pivot — the most direct way to hear it function as a passing tone across a chord change. **Blues Turnaround** ends a 12-bar phrase using the b5 to create the tension that resolves back to bar 1.

## The Blues Scale Across the Neck

The five positions of the blues scale mirror the five positions of the minor pentatonic — the b5 is simply inserted wherever it fits within the fret window. Once you know all five pentatonic positions, you already know where the blue note slots in for each one. Practice inserting it as a single passing note into phrases you already know from the pentatonic positions rather than re-learning the entire scale as a new shape.
