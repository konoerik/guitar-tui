## From Position 1 to Position 2

Position 2 begins where Position 1 ends. The lowest note of Position 2 (b3 on the low E string, fret 8) is the highest note of Position 1 on the same string. Knowing this overlap point gives you a reliable handoff between the two shapes and is the key to eventually linking all five positions across the neck.

Notice that the root notes (**■**) now sit on the D string (fret 7) and B string (fret 10), not on the low E string. In Position 1, the lowest root was on the low E at fret 5. Here it has moved to the D string. This is characteristic of Position 2 — the root sits in the middle of the shape rather than at the bottom.

## Fingering

Use the standard one-finger-per-fret approach for this position: the index finger covers fret 7, middle finger covers fret 8 (where needed), ring covers fret 9, and pinky covers fret 10. The G string is the exception — only frets 7 and 9 are used there (no fret 10), so the pinky stays unused on that string.

## Practice Approach

1. **Learn the shape slowly**, string by string from low E to high e and back.
2. **Find the root notes first** — D string fret 7 and B string fret 10. Practise starting and ending phrases on these two points.
3. **Connect to Position 1** — play the top of Position 1 (high e, fret 8, degree b3) then continue directly into Position 2. The two notes on the low E string (fret 8 and fret 10) give you the bridge.

## What This Position Sounds Like

Because the root does not sit at the bottom of the shape, phrases in Position 2 sound naturally mid-range in character. Blues players use Position 2 extensively for runs that descend from the upper strings into the root territory around the D string — the resolution has a sinking, satisfying feel. Compare playing a phrase that ends on the root at the D string (fret 7) versus the B string (fret 10) — they are both A, but the B string root has a brighter, more ringing quality.
