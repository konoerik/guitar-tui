## Notes Are Not Music Yet

You can run A minor pentatonic up and down in five positions. That is vocabulary, not speech. What turns notes into music is **phrasing** — grouping them into shaped statements that begin somewhere, go somewhere, and land somewhere.

The most useful lens for this is **contour**: the line your pitches draw. Sing any melody you love and trace it in the air — it rises, peaks, and settles. Almost every memorable phrase has a describable shape. Almost every forgettable solo doesn't.

## The Arc

The most common phrase shape in melodic playing: climb toward a single highest note, arrive on it, and come back down to rest. One peak per phrase.

## Other Shapes

The arc is one option, not a rule. The core catalog:

- **Arc** (rise–peak–fall) — the default for singable, resolved statements
- **Descent** (start high, walk down) — declarative, conclusive; the shape of many classic openings
- **Ascent** (climb and *stay* up) — builds tension; hands the energy to whatever comes next
- **Valley** (fall, then recover) — the arc inverted; sighing, reflective

The lick library is full of these: *Box 1 Ascending Run* is a pure ascent; *Natural Minor Position 4 — Classical Descent* is a descent. Revisit licks you already play and name their shapes — from now on, you should always be able to.

## How to Practice Shape

1. **Decide before you play.** Over a looper vamp (Am works), announce a shape out loud — "arc" — then play *any* notes from position 1 that trace it. The notes matter far less than you think; the commitment matters more.
2. **One peak.** Give each phrase exactly one highest note and do not touch it twice. This single constraint kills aimless noodling faster than any other exercise.
3. **Land deliberately.** End on the root for full resolution, or on the 5th to leave the door open — but *choose*.

## Common Mistakes

- **Multiple peaks** — hitting the top note, leaving, and returning to it dilutes the arrival. One summit per phrase.
- **Even note values throughout** — shape needs time as well as pitch: hold the peak, hold the landing.
- **Starting every phrase on the root** — the root is a powerful *destination*; as a permanent departure point it makes everything sound like a scale exercise. Start on the 5th or b3 sometimes.
