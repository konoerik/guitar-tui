## The Long Arc

Everything in this track so far operates at the scale of one or two bars. A solo is the same craft at the scale of a whole chorus: it needs a **shape** — and the shape that has served every genre from B.B. King to Gilmour is the long arc:

1. **Opening** — low register, few notes, lots of space. Introduce a motif.
2. **Development** — the motif returns, transposed, extended, displaced. Density rises.
3. **Peak** — the highest register and the most intensity, *once*, past the solo's midpoint.
4. **Resolution** — descend, thin out, land on the root.

This is the phrase-shape lesson's arc stretched across sixteen bars instead of four beats — the same principle at every zoom level.

## Pacing: the Resource You Spend

A solo's only scarce resource is intensity, and it is spent on three axes:

- **Register** — every step up the neck is money spent; the high notes buy less if you have been up there the whole time.
- **Density** — notes per bar. Start at two or three; the peak can hold sixteen.
- **Dynamics** — pick harder as the solo grows. Playing the peak at the opening's volume wastes it.

The most common structural mistake in amateur solos is starting at 90% on all three axes — leaving nowhere to go except more of the same. If your first bar is the fastest, highest, loudest thing you can do, the solo ended in bar one.

## The One-Peak Rule, Scaled Up

From the phrase-shape lesson: one summit per phrase. The same rule governs the whole solo — one *global* peak, placed roughly two-thirds through. Phrases before it may have local high points, but each should reach slightly higher than the last, so the solo climbs in waves. If everything is a climax, nothing is.

## Practice

1. **The three-phrase plan**: over any loop, improvise exactly three phrases — low and sparse, middle and moving, high peak resolving home. Say the plan out loud before you start. Structure imposed in advance is what turns noodling into a solo.
2. **The register budget**: improvise a full chorus where you may not touch the top two strings until the final four bars. Feel how much the arrival earns.
3. **Steal the map**: pick one recorded solo you love and chart it — where is the peak? Where is the space? How does it open? You will find the arc almost every time.

## Common Mistakes

- **Opening with fireworks** — see pacing above; the strongest openers are nearly always the simplest.
- **Multiple peaks** — three climaxes read as none. Choose the summit and protect it.
- **No ending** — trailing off is not resolving. The last phrase should land its root as deliberately as the first phrase entered.
