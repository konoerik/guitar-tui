## Small Ideas, Repeated with Purpose

Ask a beginner to improvise and they hunt for *more notes*. Ask an experienced player and they do the opposite: they find a **motif** — a short, distinct cell of two to five notes — and spend the next several bars *developing* it. Beethoven's Fifth runs on four notes. Most memorable guitar solos run on two or three ideas, developed, not dozens of ideas abandoned.

The reason is the listener. Recognition is what separates music from wandering: when an idea returns changed, the audience is rewarded for paying attention. A motif is a promise; development keeps it.

## The Seed

Note what stayed fixed: the rhythm (two short, one long), the contour (up, up), the rest. Only the pitches moved. Development changes **one thing at a time** — change everything and it is a new idea, not a developed one.

## The Four Development Moves

1. **Repeat** — play it again, exactly. Underrated and completely legitimate; repetition *is* emphasis.
2. **Transpose** — same shape, different scale degrees (the diagram above). Within one position this means sliding the cell across strings.
3. **Extend** — keep the head of the motif, grow the tail: A–C–D becomes A–C–D–E–G. The familiar opening buys attention for the new ending.
4. **Vary the rhythm** — same pitches, new placement: squeeze the motif into sixteenths, stretch it over the bar, or start it on the "&" (the next lesson is devoted to this).

A workable solo chorus can be exactly: state, repeat, transpose, extend-and-resolve. Four moves, one idea.

## Development vs. Noodling

The test is simple: could a listener hum your idea back after the second phrase? If yes, you are developing. If no — if each phrase is unrelated to the last — you are browsing the scale. Both use the same notes; only one is memorable.

This is also why motifs pair naturally with question-and-answer structure from the previous lesson: a question phrase built on a motif and an answer built on its development is the strongest two-bar unit in improvisation.

## Practice

1. Loop **Am – F – G – Am** (one bar each). Improvise a full cycle using *only* three pitches (A, C, D). The constraint forces development — with no new notes available, variation must come from rhythm, order, and space.
2. Take the **Motif and Development** lick from the Practice tab and replace its seed with your own three notes, keeping its plan (state, transpose, extend home).
3. Steal a motif from a song you love — the first three notes of any vocal melody work — and run the four moves on it in position 1.

## Common Mistakes

- **Motifs that are too long** — a seven-note "motif" is a phrase; the ear cannot hold it well enough to notice development. Two to five notes.
- **Changing rhythm and pitch at once** — the recognition thread snaps. One change per repetition.
- **Abandoning the idea too early** — the third appearance of a motif is where the listener starts enjoying the game. Most players quit after one.
