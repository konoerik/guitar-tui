## When You Play It Is What It Says

Take any phrase you know and ask one question: *which beat does the first note land on?* Most self-taught players have a single unconscious answer — beat 1, every time — and their solos inherit a squareness they can never quite name. Placement is the fix, and it costs no new notes.

Three placements cover most of the territory:

| Entry | Character | Heard in |
|-------|-----------|----------|
| On the beat (1) | Grounded, declarative | Anthem melodies, opening statements |
| Off the beat (the "&") | Leaning, propulsive | Funk lines, bebop, restless rock leads |
| Delayed (after 1, e.g. beat 2) | Patient, conversational | Blues — the pickup breath before speaking |

## Landing Is Placement Too

Entries get the attention, but *arrivals* carry the meaning. Landing your phrase's final root exactly on beat 1 of the next bar is the strongest resolution available — the melodic home and the rhythmic home arrive together. Landing it on the "&" just before beat 1 (an anticipation) resolves early and pushes the music forward; blues and rock live on this move.

A practical rule for solos: **vary your entries, protect your landings.** Enter anywhere; land somewhere deliberate.

## The Back of the Beat

One more dimension, too fine for notation: micro-placement. Two players can read this page's diagrams identically and sound completely different, because one strikes fractionally behind the metronome (relaxed, heavy — think slow blues) and one dead on it (urgent, tight). You cannot read this; you can only hear it. Record yourself with a metronome and listen to where your notes actually sit — most players are further ahead of the beat than they believe, and simply *intending* to relax behind the click audibly changes the feel.

## Practice

1. **Four entrances**: pick one short motif. Over a looped Am groove, play it entering on 1, then on the "&" of 1, then on 2, then on the "&" of 2. Same notes every time. Notice each entrance changes what the phrase seems to mean.
2. **Anticipated landings**: improvise two-bar phrases that end on A, first landing exactly on the next bar's beat 1, then landing on the "&" just before it. Alternate until both feel available on demand.
3. Play the **Displaced Cell** lick from the Practice tab daily this week — it is this entire lesson in two bars.

## Common Mistakes

- **Displacement by accident** — off-beat entries chosen deliberately groove; off-beat entries from losing count lurch. The difference is whether you can also land the on-beat version at will.
- **Every phrase syncopated** — constant off-beat playing is as square as constant on-beat playing. Contrast is the tool.
- **Ignoring the count during rests** — the entry rest in the displaced bar is where the placement happens. Count through it.
