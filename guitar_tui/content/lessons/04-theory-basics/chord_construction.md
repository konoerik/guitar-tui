## Chords Are Stacked Intervals

A chord is built by selecting specific scale degrees and playing them simultaneously. The most common method is *tertian harmony* — stacking notes a third apart. A third means skipping every other scale degree: 1–3–5, or 1–3–5–7 for four-note chords.

## The Four Basic Chord Types

### Major Chord — 1 – 3 – 5

The root (1), major 3rd (3), and perfect 5th (5). Four semitones + three semitones.

Example: **A major** = A (root) + C# (major 3rd) + E (perfect 5th)

### Minor Chord — 1 – b3 – 5

The root (1), *minor* 3rd (b3), and perfect 5th (5). Three semitones + four semitones. The only difference from a major chord is that the 3rd is one semitone lower.

Example: **A minor** = A (root) + C (minor 3rd) + E (perfect 5th)

### Dominant 7th Chord — 1 – 3 – 5 – b7

Major chord plus a minor 7th on top. The "dominant 7th" is the defining sound of blues and jazz — it creates tension that resolves strongly to the chord a fifth below.

Example: **A7** = A + C# + E + G

Major chord plus a *major* 7th — one semitone below the octave. Sounds lush, jazzy, and resolved — the b7 has been replaced by the 7.

## The Pattern

| Chord type | Formula | 3rd | 7th |
|-----------|---------|-----|-----|
| Major | 1 – 3 – 5 | major (4 semitones) | — |
| Minor | 1 – b3 – 5 | minor (3 semitones) | — |
| Dominant 7th | 1 – 3 – 5 – b7 | major | minor |
| Major 7th | 1 – 3 – 5 – 7 | major | major |
| Minor 7th | 1 – b3 – 5 – b7 | minor | minor |

The **third** (major or minor) is what determines whether a chord sounds happy or sad. The **seventh** (major or minor, if present) adds colour and complexity.

## Why Guitar Shapes Work Anywhere

On guitar, the same shape — the same relative finger positions — produces the same *chord type* regardless of where you play it, because the interval relationships between the strings are fixed. An open A major chord uses frets that spell out the intervals 1–3–5 starting from A. Move the root to a different note using a barre, and the same interval pattern now spells out 1–3–5 from that new root — a major chord in the new key.

This is why learning the *formula* of a chord (its interval structure) is more powerful than memorising the specific frets — once you understand the recipe, the shape makes sense, and transposing to any key is a matter of moving the root to the right string and fret.
