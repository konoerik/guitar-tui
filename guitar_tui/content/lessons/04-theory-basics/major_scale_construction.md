## The Major Scale Formula

The major scale is defined by a fixed sequence of whole steps (W = 2 semitones) and half steps (H = 1 semitone):

**W – W – H – W – W – W – H**

This formula always produces the same *type* of scale — the characteristic bright, settled sound most people recognise as "do-re-mi." Apply it starting from any note and you get that key's major scale.

## Building C Major

Starting on C and applying the formula:

| Step | Note | Interval from C |
|------|------|----------------|
| Start | C | 1 (root) |
| +W | D | 2 (major 2nd) |
| +W | E | 3 (major 3rd) |
| +H | F | 4 (perfect 4th) |
| +W | G | 5 (perfect 5th) |
| +W | A | 6 (major 6th) |
| +W | B | 7 (major 7th) |
| +H | C | 1 (octave) |

Notes: C D E F G A B. Interval formula: **1 – 2 – 3 – 4 – 5 – 6 – 7**.

## Building A Major

The same formula from A:

| Step | Note | Interval from A |
|------|------|----------------|
| Start | A | 1 |
| +W | B | 2 |
| +W | C# | 3 |
| +H | D | 4 |
| +W | E | 5 |
| +W | F# | 6 |
| +W | G# | 7 |
| +H | A | 1 (octave) |

Notes: A B C# D E F# G#. Three sharps.

## Why the Major Scale Matters

The major scale is the reference point for almost all Western music theory:

- **Intervals** are named relative to the major scale. A "major 3rd" means the 3rd degree of the major scale. A "minor 3rd" (b3) is one semitone below that.
- **Chords are built from the major scale** — stacking every other note produces the seven diatonic chords of the key.
- **Modes are derived from it** — each of the seven modes (Ionian, Dorian, Phrygian, etc.) is built by starting the major scale formula from a different degree.
- **The minor scale is defined relative to it** — the natural minor scale is the major scale with flattened 3rd, 6th, and 7th (b3, b6, b7).

Once the major scale formula is in your ear and hands, every other scale and chord becomes a variation or derivation from this single pattern.

## Memorising Keys

Start by learning C major (no sharps or flats — just the natural notes). Then learn G major (one sharp: F#) and F major (one flat: Bb). These three keys cover the most common guitar progressions. From there, each new key adds one sharp or flat to the previous key — a pattern described by the circle of fifths.
