## Seven Letters, Twelve Notes

Music uses only seven letter names: **A B C D E F G**. After G, the cycle begins again at A. These seven letters name the seven *natural notes* — the white keys on a piano, the notes with no sharps or flats.

Between most adjacent natural notes there is a gap of one semitone called an *accidental*:

```
A – A# – B – C – C# – D – D# – E – F – F# – G – G# – A …
```

That gives 12 unique notes total before the pattern repeats. This repeating 12-note sequence is the **chromatic scale**.

Two pairs of natural notes are naturally only one semitone apart — **E and F**, and **B and C**. There is no E# or B# in common usage; E and F are adjacent, as are B and C.

## Sharps and Flats Are the Same Notes

A **sharp** (♯) raises a note by one semitone; a **flat** (♭) lowers it by one semitone. This means most accidentals have two names:

| Name 1 | Name 2 |
|--------|--------|
| A# | Bb |
| C# | Db |
| D# | Eb |
| F# | Gb |
| G# | Ab |

These pairs are called *enharmonic equivalents* — the same pitch, different spelling. Which name to use depends on context; both are correct. Guitar charts typically use sharps when ascending (A#) and flats when descending (Bb), though this varies.

## Notes on the Low E String

Fret 12 is the octave — E again, one octave higher. From fret 13 onward the pattern repeats identically.

## Why This Matters for Guitar

Every moveable chord and scale shape has its root on a specific string. For E-shape barre chords, the root is on string 6 — so reading the note names on the low E string tells you where to play any chord in any key. Place an E-shape major barre with the lowest finger at fret 5 (A) and you have A major. Move it to fret 8 (C) and you have C major.

Memorising the natural notes on the low E and A strings — just the seven letter names without accidentals — is the single highest-leverage fretboard knowledge a guitarist can have.
