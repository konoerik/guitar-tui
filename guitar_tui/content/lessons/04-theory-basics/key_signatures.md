## What Is a Key Signature?

A **key signature** tells you which notes in the major scale are raised (sharped) or lowered (flatted) for a given key. In C major, all notes are natural — no sharps or flats. Every other key modifies some of the seven notes to maintain the W–W–H–W–W–W–H interval formula.

## Sharp Keys

Moving up by a perfect 5th from C adds one sharp each time:

| Key | Sharps | Notes sharpened |
|-----|--------|----------------|
| C | 0 | (none) |
| G | 1 | F# |
| D | 2 | F#, C# |
| A | 3 | F#, C#, G# |
| E | 4 | F#, C#, G#, D# |
| B | 5 | F#, C#, G#, D#, A# |
| F# | 6 | F#, C#, G#, D#, A#, E# |

The sharps always appear in the same order: **F# C# G# D# A# E# B#** (remembered as "Father Charles Goes Down And Ends Battle").

## Flat Keys

Moving down by a perfect 5th from C adds one flat each time:

| Key | Flats | Notes flattened |
|-----|-------|----------------|
| C | 0 | (none) |
| F | 1 | Bb |
| Bb | 2 | Bb, Eb |
| Eb | 3 | Bb, Eb, Ab |
| Ab | 4 | Bb, Eb, Ab, Db |
| Db | 5 | Bb, Eb, Ab, Db, Gb |
| Gb | 6 | Bb, Eb, Ab, Db, Gb, Cb |

The flats appear in the same order: **Bb Eb Ab Db Gb Cb Fb** — the reverse of the sharps order ("Battle Ends And Down Goes Charles' Father").

## Guitar-Friendly Keys

Most guitar music uses keys with few sharps or flats, because open-string voicings only work cleanly in keys that include the open string notes (E, A, D, G, B):

| Key | Sharps/flats | Common guitar use |
|-----|-------------|------------------|
| E major | 4# | "native" guitar key — all open strings harmonise |
| A major | 3# | extremely common for rock and blues |
| D major | 2# | many folk and country songs |
| G major | 1# | the most common beginner key |
| C major | 0 | the "piano-friendly" key, very common |
| Bb major | 2b | common for horn players, needs capo for guitarists |

## The Circle of Fifths

The 12 major keys arranged by fifth-relationships form a circle. Moving clockwise adds a sharp; moving counter-clockwise adds a flat. Adjacent keys on the circle share six of their seven notes — they sound closely related.

```
           C (0)
      F(1b)   G(1#)
   Bb(2b)       D(2#)
  Eb(3b)         A(3#)
   Ab(4b)       E(4#)
      Db(5b)  B(5#)
           Gb/F#(6)
```

## Why This Matters for Guitar

**Transposing.** If you know a song in G and someone asks for it in A, you are moving two positions clockwise on the circle (adding two sharps). Every note shifts up by a major 2nd (two frets).

**Chord relationships.** Keys adjacent on the circle share chords. G and D are neighbours — they share the A minor chord. Many progressions that sound natural together (G–D–Em–C, for example) are built from keys clustered on the same arc of the circle.

**Capo use.** A capo effectively moves your key without changing your chord shapes. Capo at fret 2 and play G shapes → sounds in A. The capo transposes you clockwise by the number of semitones.
