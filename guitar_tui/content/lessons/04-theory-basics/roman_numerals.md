## What Are Roman Numerals in Music?

Roman numerals label chords by their *position in a key* rather than by their specific note names. Instead of saying "play G, then C, then D," you say "play I, then IV, then V in the key of G." The Roman numeral describes the chord's role in the key, which stays constant even when you transpose to a different key.

Uppercase numerals (I, IV, V) indicate major chords. Lowercase (ii, iii, vi, vii°) indicate minor or diminished chords.

## The Diatonic Chords of the Major Scale

Building a chord on each degree of the major scale — using only notes from that scale — produces seven chords, one per scale degree:

| Degree | Numeral | Chord type | In C major | In G major |
|--------|---------|-----------|-----------|-----------|
| 1 | **I** | major | C | G |
| 2 | **ii** | minor | Dm | Am |
| 3 | **iii** | minor | Em | Bm |
| 4 | **IV** | major | F | C |
| 5 | **V** | major | G | D |
| 6 | **vi** | minor | Am | Em |
| 7 | **vii°** | diminished | B° | F#° |

The pattern — major, minor, minor, major, major, minor, diminished — is always the same in any major key. Only the specific note names change.

## The Most Common Progressions

The most prevalent progressions in popular music are combinations of I, IV, V, and vi:

| Numeral pattern | In G major | In A major |
|----------------|-----------|-----------|
| I – IV – V | G – C – D | A – D – E |
| I – V – vi – IV | G – D – Em – C | A – E – F#m – D |
| vi – IV – I – V | Em – C – G – D | F#m – D – A – E |
| I – IV – vi – V | G – C – Em – D | A – D – F#m – E |

Notice that the I–V–vi–IV and vi–IV–I–V progressions use the same four chords in a different order — thousands of songs are built from these same four Roman numerals.

## Transposing with Roman Numerals

The power of Roman numerals is transposition. If someone says "the song is I–IV–V in A," you immediately know the chords are A–D–E without having to rederive anything. If the singer needs it in G instead, you look up I–IV–V in G: G–C–D. The relationship stays constant.

Chord charts written in Roman numerals are key-independent — they describe the *structure* of a song, not just one specific version of it.

## Minor Key Notation

In minor keys, the tonic chord is lowercase (i — a minor chord). The most common minor key progression is **i – VII – VI – VII** or **i – VI – III – VII**:

| Numeral | In A minor |
|---------|-----------|
| i | Am |
| VI | F |
| III | C |
| VII | G |

The lowercase numerals indicate minor chords; uppercase indicate major chords borrowed from the parallel or relative major. This is the basis of the Am–F–C–G progression heard in countless rock and pop songs.
