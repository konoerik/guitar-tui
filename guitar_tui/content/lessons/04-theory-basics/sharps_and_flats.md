## Moving by Semitones

A **semitone** is the smallest step in standard Western music — one fret on the guitar. A **tone** (or *whole step*) is two semitones — two frets.

A **sharp** (♯) raises any note by one semitone. A **flat** (♭) lowers any note by one semitone. Applied to natural notes:

- E raised one semitone → F (not E#, because E and F are naturally adjacent)
- B raised one semitone → C (same reason)
- C lowered one semitone → B (not Cb in common usage)
- F lowered one semitone → E

## Enharmonic Equivalents

The five accidentals each have two names. The pitch is identical; the name depends on context:

| Sharp | Flat | Common context |
|-------|------|----------------|
| A# | Bb | Bb more common in major keys |
| C# | Db | C# in sharp keys, Db in flat keys |
| D# | Eb | Eb more common in guitar |
| F# | Gb | F# more common |
| G# | Ab | Ab common in flat key signatures |

In practical guitar work, you will mostly see **Bb, Eb, Ab** (flats) and **F#, C#** (sharps) — this matches the most guitar-friendly keys. When a friend says "play the B-flat chord," you look for fret 1 on the A string (Bb) and use an A-shape barre shape.

## The Two Natural Semitones

Only two pairs of natural notes sit one semitone apart:

- **E → F**: no sharp between them. Moving from open E string to fret 1 gives F.
- **B → C**: no sharp between them. String 2 open is B; fret 1 is C.

Every other pair of natural notes has a semitone gap between them (a sharp/flat note exists between A and B, between C and D, between D and E, between F and G, between G and A).

## Why This Matters

Understanding enharmonics prevents confusion when chord charts, scale diagrams, and keys use different names for the same pitch. When you see "Gb major," knowing that Gb = F# tells you to look at fret 2 on the low E string — the same physical position you'd use for an F# major chord. The name changes; the guitar shape does not.
