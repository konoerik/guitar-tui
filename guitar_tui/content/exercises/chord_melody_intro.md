## The Exercise

**Chord melody** means one guitar does both jobs: the melody note is always the *highest* sounding string, and chord tones ring below it. The arrangement below sets the opening of Beethoven's *Ode to Joy* in C major — strike a full chord where marked, then let it ring while the single melody notes continue on top.

Bar by bar: **C** with the open high e (melody E) on top; **C with G on top** (pinky reaches fret 3 while the shape holds); **C with C on top** (high e stays out — the B-string C is now the highest voice); **G with D on top** closing the phrase on a half cadence — loop back to bar 1 and it resolves.

## Rules

- **The melody rules the arrangement.** Every beat, the highest sounding pitch must be the tune. If a chord tone rings above the melody note, mute it or leave it out — that is why bar 3's high e is silent.
- **Let ring.** The chord struck on beat 1 sustains under the following melody notes; lift nothing you don't have to.
- **Melody louder than harmony**: pick (or thumb-strum) the beat-1 chord gently, then give the single notes a firmer attack. The tune must sing over the accompaniment.
- Play it rubato first — no metronome — until the shapes and reaches are automatic. Then in time at 60 BPM.

## Progression

1. Melody alone (high e and B strings) until it is fluent — you cannot harmonize a tune you cannot play.
2. As written, chords on beat 1 only.
3. Re-harmonize with sevenths: swap bar 1's C for Cmaj7 (`x32000`) and bar 4's G for G7 — hear the color change under the identical melody.
4. Arrange your own: take any simple melody in C on the top two strings and put C, F, G, Am shapes under it, top note always the tune. This is the whole craft of solo guitar in miniature.
