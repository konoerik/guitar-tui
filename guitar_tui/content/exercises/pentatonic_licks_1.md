## The Exercise

Running a pentatonic scale up and down is not improvisation. Improvisation is combining short phrases — licks — with intention. These five phrases all live inside A minor pentatonic position 1. Each one is four beats and targets a specific melodic idea: descending, ascending, root grip, low end, and resolution. Learn each one until it feels automatic, then combine them freely.

**Lick 1 — Descend to Root**: G string down to high e, landing on A. Starts from the 4th (D), steps through b3 (C) and 5th (E), resolves to root. The most natural "ending phrase."

**Lick 2 — Ascending**: D string up through G string, ending on the 4th. Starts from b7 (G), moves to root (A), b3 (C), 4th (D). A bright, ascending call.

**Lick 3 — Root Grip**: Hovers around the root on the high e. Leaves from A (root), reaches to C (b3), grabs G (b7), returns to A. A short circular phrase — the sound of "settling on the tonic."

**Lick 4 — Low End**: Strings 6 and 5, the "heavy" version. Root (A) → b3 (C) → 4th (D) → 5th (E). An ascending low-register phrase with weight and drive.

**Lick 5 — Resolution**: Same contour as Lick 1 but with a longer hold. G string C → E on B string → A root. The two-beat A at the end makes this a clear ending phrase, not a connector.

## Rules

- Learn each lick at **70 BPM** until it is completely automatic — no hesitation between notes.
- Use **a metronome**. The rhythmic placement of each note matters as much as the pitch. Land the last note of each lick precisely on the beat.
- Practice **one lick per session** until it is memorized, then add the next. Do not move on until the current lick requires no conscious thought.

## Combining Licks

Once all five are automatic, improvise using only these phrases:
- Play Lick 4 (low end), then Lick 1 (descend to root) — call and response
- Play Lick 2 (ascending) then Lick 5 (resolution) — a complete two-phrase statement
- Repeat any lick with variation: transpose it to a different part of the position, or change the duration of the last note

The goal is not to play more notes — it is to make musical statements with the notes you have.
