## The Exercise

Rather than ascending or descending the scale note-by-note, you play each
group of three consecutive notes before stepping forward by one. Starting on
A (fret 5, low E string):

- Play notes 1, 2, 3 — then step forward
- Play notes 2, 3, 4 — then step forward
- Play notes 3, 4, 5 — and so on

This creates overlapping groups with a built-in rhythmic feel.

After completing the ascent through position 1, reverse and descend in the
same groups-of-three pattern.

## Rules

- Use a metronome. Start at **60 BPM**, triplets (three notes per beat).
- Each group of three is one beat — the accent falls naturally on the first
  note of each group.
- Use strict alternate picking throughout (down-up-down, up-down-up).
- Once comfortable ascending, practice descending. Then combine: ascend
  through position 1, cross into position 2, and continue.

## Why It Works

Running a scale straight up and down is rarely musical. Sequences impose
rhythmic and melodic structure on the same notes — the overlapping groups
create a sense of forward motion and natural phrase endings. Almost every
recognizable blues and rock lick is built from a sequence applied to a
pentatonic position. Mastering sequences of three is the first step toward
hearing those licks not as patterns to memorize but as consequences of a
simple rule applied to a scale you already know.

## Progression

Once sequences of three feel automatic:
1. Apply the same principle to sequences of four (1-2-3-4, 2-3-4-5, etc.)
2. Apply to natural minor and major scale positions
3. Mix ascending sequences with descending phrases within the same run
