## The Exercise

Play all four 7th chord qualities rooted on A, one at a time, in this order:
Amaj7 → A7 → Am7 → Am7b5. Hold each chord for four slow beats, then move
to the next. Arpeggiate each chord before strumming to confirm every note
rings.

Cycle: Amaj7 → A7 → Am7 → Am7b5 → Amaj7. Repeat 4–6 times at a slow,
deliberate tempo. Then move the same cycle to D (same shapes, root on the
D string).

## What to listen for

The differences between these chords are small — often a single note changes
between each step. Train yourself to hear each change:

- **Amaj7 → A7**: the major 7th (G#) drops to the flat 7th (G). The chord
  loses its settled, floating quality and gains a slight tension — the
  dominant pull.
- **A7 → Am7**: the major 3rd (C#) drops to the minor 3rd (C). The chord
  shifts from bright-with-tension to dark-with-tension.
- **Am7 → Am7b5**: the perfect 5th (E) drops to the flat 5th (Eb). The chord
  loses its stability — this is the most unstable of the four.

## Rules

- No metronome. This is an ear-training and voicing exercise.
- Arpeggiate each chord fully before moving to the next.
- Name each chord quality aloud as you play it. The verbal label reinforces
  the ear-to-theory connection.
- After the A cycle, transpose to D, G, and E roots.

## Progression

Once the cycle is internalized by ear:
1. Play the cycle and identify each chord quality *without* looking at the
   diagram — form the shape from memory.
2. Play two-chord vamps: Amaj7 → Dmaj7 (jazz ballad feel), A7 → D7 (blues),
   Am7 → Dm7 (minor groove). Notice how the 7th quality affects the
   progression's character.
3. Combine with the diatonic chord strip in the Reference screen: select a
   key and locate which 7th qualities appear naturally on each degree.
