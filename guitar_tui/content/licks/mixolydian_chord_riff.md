## The Lick

This phrase works like a classic rock riff: it starts on the root, outlines the chord (G B D), rises to the sixth and then steps to the flat seventh (F natural), resolves back down through C and B, and returns to the root. The F natural at beat 5 is the defining moment — in G major that note would be F#, but G Mixolydian has F natural, and that one semitone difference is what makes the riff feel like *Sweet Home Alabama* rather than a straight major phrase.

**G Mixolydian: G A B C D E F. This phrase uses G B D E F C B G — outlining the G triad then stepping through the major 6th (E) to the b7 (F), walking back through C and B to the root. The F natural (beat 5) would be F# in G major — that half-step difference is the entire sound of the mode.**

## Looper Setup

Set up: G – F, two bars each.

- **Tempo**: 90–110 BPM
- **Feel**: Rhythmic, strummed right-hand attack — treat this as a riff, not a scale run. Drive each note with a deliberate downstroke.
- **Phrasing**: Start on beat 1 of the G bar. The F natural lands on beat 1 of the second bar, directly as the F chord arrives — the b7 becomes the root of the chord, which resolves the tension perfectly. The phrase then walks back to G, landing on the root just before the loop repeats.

## Why This Works

The G–F two-chord vamp is the harmonic engine of countless classic rock songs — it works because both chords are diatonic to G Mixolydian and neither is a minor chord, giving the whole thing a driving, unresolved major feeling that sits between blues and rock. The phrase treats the F as a goal note rather than a passing note: it arrives with emphasis and stays for a full beat, then the descent back to G provides resolution.

This is a "chord-riff hybrid" because the first three beats outline the G chord (root, third, fifth) in the same way a rhythm guitarist might voice a riff over a chord — the melody and the harmony imply the same thing simultaneously.

## Technique Notes

- **The D string open (beat 3)**: the phrase jumps from A string fret 2 to D string open. Let the open D ring clearly — it is the 5th of G major and sits well against the chord.
- **The F (D string fret 3, beat 5)**: play this with deliberate emphasis — it is the Mixolydian fingerprint. A slight accent here tells the ear this is not a G major phrase.
- **Tempo variation**: at 70 BPM the phrase has a bluesy, laid-back feel. At 120 BPM it has the momentum of a driving rock riff. Same notes, completely different character — experiment with both extremes.
- **Extension**: once comfortable, add palm muting to beats 1 and 8 (the root notes) and let beats 3–7 ring open. This gives the riff the percussive attack of classic rock rhythm playing.
