## The Lick

One four-note cell (A–C–D–E), two placements. Bar 1 starts squarely on beat 1: grounded, declarative. Bar 2 waits an eighth note and starts on the "&" of 1: the identical notes now float across the beat, each one landing where the ear expected silence.

**Both bars: A C D E as eighth notes. Bar 1 enters on beat 1; bar 2 enters on the "&" of 1.** Nothing else changes — which is exactly why the difference is so audible.

## Looper Setup

Record **Am** for two bars at 75–85 BPM and loop. Alternate the two placements bar by bar. Keep counting "1 & 2 & 3 & 4 &" aloud — in bar 2 every note falls on an "&".

## What to Listen For

The on-beat version sounds like a statement; the displaced version sounds like it is chasing the beat, leaning forward. Great soloists move phrases like this deliberately — the same lick can open a solo on the beat and reappear later displaced, sounding fresh both times. This is the cheapest variety in music: no new notes required.

## Technique Notes

- **Picking stays alternate** in both bars, but notice it flips: on-beat notes take downstrokes in bar 1; in bar 2 the same notes arrive on up-slots. Let the subdivision decide the stroke, not the melody.
- **The entry rest in bar 2 is the hard part** — resting on a downbeat while the loop plays takes real attention. Feel beat 1 pass, then enter.
- **Even eighths throughout**: displacement only reads clearly if the cell's internal rhythm is identical both times.
