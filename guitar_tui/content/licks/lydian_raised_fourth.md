## The Lick

Six notes ascending from the G root up through A, B, C# (the raised fourth),
D, and resolving to E on the B string. The C# (D string, fret 11 or G string
fret 6) is the defining note — in G major it would be C natural.

**G Lydian = G A B C# D E F#. The C# (where C natural would be in G major) is the raised fourth — the note that gives Lydian its characteristic lift and brightness.**

## Looper Setup

Record **G – A**, two bars each, at a moderate tempo (85–95 BPM). This I – II
movement is the Lydian fingerprint: in G major, the II chord would be Am
(minor). A major chord here exists because the raised fourth (C#) makes A
major a diatonic chord in G Lydian. When A major arrives, the C# is its major
third — the note you just emphasized locks perfectly into the chord.

Over G: the run ascends naturally through the tonic chord.
Over A: the C# you land on is the major third of A — the phrase resolves
rather than clashing.

## What to Listen For

The C# is the pivot. When you play it over the G chord, it sounds slightly
lifted — not dissonant, but not fully settled. When the A chord arrives, that
same note becomes a strong chord tone and the tension releases. This
consonance-on-arrival is what makes Lydian phrases sound like they are
*landing* from above rather than resolving from below.

## Technique Notes

This phrase works best at a moderate, singing tempo — let each note sustain
rather than rushing to the next. The ascending motion suits clean tone with
some reverb. Slide into the C# from C natural (one fret below) to emphasize
its raised quality — the slide makes the alteration audible.
