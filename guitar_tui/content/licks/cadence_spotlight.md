## The Lick

The one-note, one-chord move isolated: nothing in this phrase is exotic
except the G# (D string, fret 6), and it is saved for the exact moment the E
chord can support it.

**Over Am: E – D – C – A, pure pentatonic. Over E: the G# is the chord's
major 3rd and the key's leading tone — held two beats, then released up a
half step to A.**

## Looper Setup

Record **Am – E**, one bar each, at 90 BPM. Aim the final A to sound just as
the loop cycles back to the Am bar — leading tone resolving to root exactly
on the chord change. If the timing slips, slow the loop down; the arrival is
the entire lick.

## Why This Works

Bar one establishes ordinary minor-key business — the ear settles. Then the
harmony turns to E major, whose G# is a half step below the key's root, and
the melody agrees with the chord instead of fighting it. Held, the G# carries
all the cadence's tension; resolved on the downbeat, it releases it. This is
the i–V–i pull of harmonic minor reduced to its single defining note.

## Technique Notes

Second finger on the G# so the third finger takes the A at fret 7 without a
shift. Commit to the G# — full duration, a touch of vibrato, no hedging. A
timid G# reads as a wrong note; a confident one reads as the smartest note in
the phrase. Those are the only two options.
