## The Lick

A descending figure through A minor pentatonic position 1 that resolves on E7
in the last bar of a 12-bar blues. The phrase moves from the high e string
down through the B and G strings, landing on A (fret 5, low E string) before
a pull-off to the open E — the V chord arrival.

**This figure spans bars 11–12 of the 12-bar blues — the "turnaround" that sends the progression back to bar 1 (I7).**

## Looper Setup

Record the full 12-bar blues in A at a slow tempo (60–70 BPM):

- Bars 1–4: A7
- Bars 5–6: D7
- Bars 7–8: A7
- Bar 9: E7
- Bar 10: D7
- Bar 11: A7
- Bar 12: E7 (turnaround bar)

Play the phrase in bars 11–12. The descent starts over A7 in bar 11 and
resolves the E note over E7 in bar 12. When bar 12 ends, you return to A7 at
bar 1 — the turnaround has done its job.

## What to Listen For

The phrase descends in contrary motion to the rising tension of the V7 chord.
That pull — descending melody over a harmonically rising moment — is what
creates the "return to the top" feeling. The final A note (or open E) hangs as
the V7 chord rings, then the I7 arrives to resolve it. This tension-and-release
across the bar line is the entire emotional logic of the blues form.

## Technique Notes

The descending motion benefits from a slight rhythmic lilt — place the first
note on beat 3 of bar 11 so the phrase arrives naturally on beat 1 of bar 12.
Once the basic shape is automatic, add a quarter-step bend to the G note at
the start of the phrase to add a blues inflection. Many players also add a
slide or hammer-on between the last two notes (A string fret 5 to open E) for
forward momentum into the next chorus.
