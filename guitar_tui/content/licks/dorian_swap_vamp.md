## The Lick

One bar of neutral pentatonic motion over Am, then the payoff: the F#
(B string, fret 7) held right as the D chord lands — the chord's own major
3rd, arriving with the chord.

**A Dorian = A B C D E F# G. Every note in bar one is plain minor pentatonic;
the F# in bar two is the single note that makes this Dorian.**

## Looper Setup

Record **Am – D**, one bar each, at 90 BPM with a light funk feel. The lick
is two bars — play it, rest two bars, play it again. Precision beats density
here: the F# must land together with the D chord, not near it.

## Why This Works

The backing casts the vote from the lessons: a major IV chord means the key's
sixth is F#, not F. Bar one deliberately avoids both sixths — pure pentatonic,
committing to nothing. Then the D chord arrives and the phrase agrees with it
instantly, F# on the downbeat. Try replacing the F# with F natural (fret 6)
once: against the D chord's own F# the clash is immediate and unmistakable.
That wrong version is the fastest ear training this vamp offers.

## Technique Notes

Third finger on the F# lets the phrase fall back to E and D without shifting.
Give the held F# a small vibrato — it is the color note and deserves the
attention — and keep bar one's notes short and even so the arrival contrasts.
