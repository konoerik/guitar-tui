## The Lick

A seven-note climb from the low root to the octave, passing straight through the
scale's signature leap: b6 to 7 (F to G#), three frets of pitch in a single step.

**A harmonic minor = A B C D E F G#. The G# (D string fret 6) is the raised 7th —
play it against the E chord and it is the chord's major 3rd.**

## Looper Setup

Record **Am – E**, two bars each, at a moderate 100 BPM. Time the run so the
F → G# → A arrival lands as the loop returns to Am — tension peaking on the E
chord, resolving exactly on the downbeat.

## Why This Works

Over Am, the opening A–B–C states plain minor. Over E, the F and G# are the
chord's b9 color and major 3rd — maximum pull. The final A is both the key's
root and the resolution of the G# leading tone a half step below it. This is
the i–V–i cadence compressed into a single melodic line.

## Technique Notes

Finger the F → G# jump as a position shift, not a stretch: first finger on F
(A string, fret 8), then shift the hand so the second finger takes G# (D string,
fret 6). Classical and gypsy players *lean* on the augmented 2nd — do not rush
through it; it is the whole point of the phrase.
