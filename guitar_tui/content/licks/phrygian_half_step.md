## The Lick

Five notes descending through E Phrygian, opening on the root (E), stepping
up to F (the b2), then resolving back down through D and B to a low E. The
F natural on fret 1 of the low E string is the Phrygian fingerprint — it is
a half step above the root, which in E major would be F#.

**E Phrygian = E F G A B C D. The F natural (A string fret 8, or low E string fret 1) is the b2 — it would be F# in E major.**

## Looper Setup

Record **E5 – F5**, two bars each, at a slow, heavy tempo (70–80 BPM works
well). This is the opening move of many metal and flamenco progressions. The
F5 chord is the bII — it exists because F natural is in the scale. The root
chord is E minor, not E major, because the scale has a minor third (G natural).

Over E5: every note in the phrase is consonant.
Over F5: E becomes a leading tone back to F, and D becomes the major 6th — the
phrase resolves naturally when F5 arrives.

## What to Listen For

The half-step movement E → F is the sound of Phrygian. No other common mode
has a half step between the root and the second degree. That friction — the
dissonance between tonic and b2 — is what gives the mode its dark, coiled
quality. Landing on F creates tension; returning to E releases it.

## Technique Notes

Play the phrase with a heavy, deliberate attack. This is not a fast lick — the
character comes from the notes themselves, not from speed. Try a slight palm
mute on the low notes to add weight. The phrase repeats naturally; let it loop
over the backing as a motif rather than a one-time run.
