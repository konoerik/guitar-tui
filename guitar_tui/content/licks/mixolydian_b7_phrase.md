## The Lick

A short phrase over A Mixolydian that opens on the root, walks up through
the major scale, and lands on the b7 (G natural). The G natural on the high e
string (fret 3) or B string (fret 8) is the defining note — in A major it
would be G#.

**A Mixolydian = A B C# D E F# G. The G natural (where G# would be in A major) is the b7 — the note that gives this mode its slightly bluesy, unresolved quality even though the tonic chord is major.**

## Looper Setup

Record **A – G**, two bars each, at a rock tempo (90–110 BPM). This I – bVII
move is everywhere in classic rock: *Sweet Home Alabama*, *Free Fallin'*,
*Hey Jude*. The G chord exists because G natural is diatonic to A Mixolydian.
If you played A major (Ionian), you would need to avoid G natural entirely;
in Mixolydian it is a chord tone.

Over A: the phrase outlines an A major sound with the G as a leading pull.
Over G: the G natural you land on is the root of the chord — the phrase
resolves perfectly as G arrives.

## What to Listen For

The G natural is the tension note over A and the resolution note over G. This
is the Mixolydian characteristic: the b7 sounds unresolved over the tonic
chord, which creates forward motion, and then resolves when the bVII chord
arrives. The phrase neither fully settles on A nor feels dark — it has a
rolling, continuous quality that fits groove-based rock.

## Technique Notes

Play this phrase with some pick attack — the b7 landing benefits from
emphasis. Try it at different tempos: slow (70 BPM) it sounds bluesy and
deliberate; fast (120 BPM) it has the momentum of a classic rock fill. Once
comfortable, try starting the phrase on the G chord beat rather than the A
beat and hear how the note relationships reverse.
