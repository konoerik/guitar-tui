## The Lick

This phrase descends through the G major scale on the top three strings, from A down to the root G. The journey covers seven distinct scale tones — including the major 6th (E) and major 7th (F#) that are absent from the major pentatonic — and lands on the root with a sense of arrival that only the full major scale can deliver.

## Looper Setup

Set up: G – D – Em – C, one bar each (the I–V–vi–IV progression).

- **Tempo**: 80–100 BPM
- **Feel**: Flowing eighth notes with a bright, resolved quality
- **Phrasing**: Start on the A (high e, fret 5) on beat 1 of the G bar. The D string G at the end lands on beat 1 of the repeat — arriving home exactly on the I chord.

## Why This Works

The phrase moves from the major 2nd (A) down to the root (G), then bounces back up through the major 6th (E), major 7th (F#), and root before descending through the chord tones (B, A) to land on G in the lower octave. The F#–G half-step is the key moment: that semitone pull is what makes major scale phrases sound resolved and "finished" rather than modal or ambiguous.

Compare this to the major pentatonic country walk in this library — that phrase avoids F# and C entirely, giving it a lighter, floating quality. This phrase uses both, which is why it sounds more grounded.

## Technique Notes

- **Hand position**: anchor the first finger at fret 2. Reach to fret 5 (B string) and fret 8 (B string) — the fret 8 reach is the only stretch.
- **The F# (B string fret 7)**: this is the note to listen for. Play it slightly louder than the surrounding notes to emphasise the leading-tone effect.
- **The resolution**: let the D string G (fret 5) ring for its full two beats. The phrase earns that pause.
