## The Lick

Six notes climbing in even whole steps, ending deliberately in mid-air on the
b7. The phrase does not resolve; that is the assignment.

**C whole tone = C D E F# G# Bb. Every step is identical — two frets — so the
line has motion but no gravity. It stops on Bb — the b7 — and hangs there,
because the scale offers nowhere more final to land.**

## Looper Setup

Record a single **C7** chord, one strum per bar, letting it ring — 80 BPM,
four bars. If your chord vocabulary reaches it, voice C7#5 (raise the G a
fret) so the backing agrees with the scale completely; with a plain C7, the
scale's G# will rub against the chord's G — a rub that is part of the
impressionist sound.

## Why This Works

Over a dominant 7th, the whole tone scale supplies the 3rd (E) and b7 (Bb) —
the chord's skeleton — plus the 9 (D), #11 (F#), and #5 (G#): every available
altered color at once. Nothing clashes because nothing settles. Monk used this
exact rising gesture as a signature; Debussy built whole pieces on the
principle.

## Technique Notes

Even attack, even duration — the line should sound mechanical in the best way,
like a stair climb with no landing. Resist adding vibrato until the final Bb,
then let it hang. Try continuing the pattern up two more frets: the shape
repeats, because the scale does.
