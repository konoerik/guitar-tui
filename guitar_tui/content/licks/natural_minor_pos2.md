## The Lick

This phrase descends through the position's most distinctive notes — the minor 6th (F) and the major 2nd (B) — the two tones that pentatonic playing leaves out. Hearing them here explains why natural minor sounds fuller and more "classical" than pure pentatonic phrasing.

## Looper Setup

Set up: Am – G – F – E, one bar each.

- **Tempo**: 70–85 BPM
- **Feel**: Eighth notes with a slightly halting, expressive quality; push slightly into the F
- **Phrasing**: Start on the G (minor 7th, A string fret 10) on beat 1 of the Am bar. The phrase resolves to A (B string fret 10) on beat 1 of the E bar — a strong resolution over the V chord.

## Why This Works

The phrase opens on G (the minor 7th) — a dark, unresolved sound — then steps down through F and E before falling to the root A on the D string. The ascent through B, D, and E that follows uses the major 2nd (B) to add momentum before the final resolve to A on the B string. The F–E half-step and the B below a C — these are the fingerprints of natural minor.

## Technique Notes

- **Hand position**: anchor the first finger at fret 7. Reach back with the third finger for fret 9, and back further with the fourth for fret 10.
- **The F (fret 8, A string)**: give this note a tiny emphasis — it is the most distinctive note in the phrase and the one that most clearly signals "natural minor" to a listener's ear.
- **Vibrato**: apply gentle vibrato on the final A (B string fret 10) to let the resolution breathe.
